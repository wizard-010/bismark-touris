The History of Rabaul in World War II

On January 23, 1942, Japanese forces captured Rabaul after fierce fighting. Over 1,000 Australian soldiers and hundreds of local troops became prisoners of war—many would later perish in the sinking of the Montevideo Maru. Rabaul became Japan's largest military base in the South Pacific, with over 100,000 troops, five airfields, and an extensive network of tunnels.

From 1943 onward, Rabaul was subjected to almost daily Allied bombing—the longest sustained air campaign of the Pacific War. Japanese forces officially surrendered on September 6, 1945, aboard the British aircraft carrier HMS Glory in Simpson Harbour.

Today, the legacy of these events is preserved in the memorials, cemeteries, and tunnels that dot the landscape—a solemn reminder of the courage and sacrifice of all who served. Our WWII tours in Papua New Guinea honor their memory.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are the Japanese tunnels safe to enter?

Yes, we only visit tunnels that have been inspected and deemed safe. Hard hats are provided and required. Your guide will brief you on safety procedures before entry.

Can I visit the grave of a specific soldier?

Absolutely. If you know the name of a soldier buried at Bitapaka War Cemetery, we can help you locate the grave. For family history research, we recommend providing details in advance.

What is the Montevideo Maru?

The Montevideo Maru was a Japanese transport ship sunk in 1942, carrying over 1,000 Australian POWs and civilians captured at Rabaul. It remains Australia's worst maritime disaster. We can include this history in your tour.
