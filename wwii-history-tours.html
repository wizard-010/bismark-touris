WWII History Tours in Papua New Guinea: Explore Rabaul's Pacific War Heritage

Discover the Pacific War History of Papua New Guinea

Rabaul was the most significant Japanese military base in the South Pacific during World War II. Our WWII history tours take you deep into this fascinating past, exploring Japanese tunnels, coastal defense guns, aircraft wrecks, and the solemn Bitapaka War Cemetery. Led by local historians who have dedicated their lives to preserving these stories, you'll gain a profound understanding of the Pacific War and its impact on Papua New Guinea.

Whether you're tracing a family member's wartime service or simply passionate about military history travel, our guided history tours offer an authentic, respectful, and deeply moving experience.

The History of Rabaul in World War II

On January 23, 1942, Japanese forces captured Rabaul after fierce fighting. Over 1,000 Australian soldiers and hundreds of local troops became prisoners of war—many would later perish in the sinking of the Montevideo Maru. Rabaul became Japan's largest military base in the South Pacific, with over 100,000 troops, five airfields, and an extensive network of tunnels.

From 1943 onward, Rabaul was subjected to almost daily Allied bombing—the longest sustained air campaign of the Pacific War. Japanese forces officially surrendered on September 6, 1945, aboard the British aircraft carrier HMS Glory in Simpson Harbour.

Today, the legacy of these events is preserved in the memorials, cemeteries, and tunnels that dot the landscape—a solemn reminder of the courage and sacrifice of all who served.
