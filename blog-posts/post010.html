The sun was just beginning to paint the Bismarck Sea in shades of gold and pink as our liveaboard vessel slipped its mooring at Walindi Plantation Resort. I stood on the deck, coffee in hand, watching the silhouette of Kimbe Bay's fringing reefs emerge from the morning mist. Behind us, the volcanoes of West New Britain rose like sleeping giants. Below us, a world more vibrant, more diverse, more alive than anything on land awaited.

I have dived in many places over my years as a guide in Papua New Guinea. I have descended into the crystal waters of Milne Bay, where pygmy seahorses cling to sea fans no larger than my thumbnail. I have drifted along the walls of the Witu Islands, where schools of barracuda spiral in silver tornadoes. I have explored the wrecks of Japanese ships in Kimbe Bay, their decks now gardens of soft coral, their hulls homes for turtles and sharks. But nothing compares to the experience of a liveaboard—of waking up each morning to a new dive site, of spending your days beneath the surface and your nights sharing stories with fellow divers under a canopy of stars.

Papua New Guinea sits at the heart of the Coral Triangle, the global epicenter of marine biodiversity. This is the Amazon of the ocean, a place where scientists estimate more than 2,000 species of fish and 600 species of coral thrive in waters that are still largely unexplored, still pristine, still wild. And the best way to experience this underwater Eden is from a liveaboard vessel—your floating base camp for the adventure of a lifetime.

In this guide, I will share everything I have learned about PNG liveaboard diving. The best vessels, the premier regions, the marine life you can expect to encounter, the logistics of planning your trip, and the tips that will make your experience unforgettable. Whether you are a seasoned diver with hundreds of dives under your belt or a beginner looking for your first big adventure, this guide will help you navigate the incredible world of Papua New Guinea liveaboard diving.

Why Papua New Guinea is a World-Class Diving Destination

Papua New Guinea occupies a unique position in the marine world. Located at the heart of the Coral Triangle—the region of the Pacific Ocean with the highest concentration of marine biodiversity on earth—PNG's waters are home to an astonishing array of life. Scientists have documented more than 2,000 species of fish, 600 species of coral, and countless invertebrates, many of which are found nowhere else on the planet.

But biodiversity is only part of the story. What makes PNG truly special is the combination of factors that create the perfect diving environment:

  • Pristine Reefs: Unlike many parts of Southeast Asia and the Caribbean, PNG's reefs remain largely untouched by mass tourism, overfishing, and coral bleaching. The underwater landscapes are as healthy and vibrant as they have been for centuries.
  • Varied Diving: PNG offers an incredible diversity of dive experiences—from gentle muck diving where you search for rare critters in sandy bottoms, to exhilarating drift dives along walls, to wrecks from World War II that have become artificial reefs teeming with life.
  • Remote Locations: Many of PNG's best dive sites are accessible only by liveaboard, meaning you'll have these underwater paradises almost entirely to yourself. It's not uncommon to spend an entire week without seeing another dive boat.
  • Cultural Richness: The same cultural diversity that defines PNG on land extends to the sea. Local communities have lived in harmony with these waters for thousands of years, and visiting villages is an integral part of many liveaboard itineraries.

What is a Liveaboard Diving Trip?

A liveaboard is a vessel that serves as your floating accommodation, dive base, and transportation for the duration of your trip. Instead of returning to a resort each evening, you sleep on the boat, wake up at the dive sites, and maximize your time in the water. Most PNG liveaboards accommodate between 8 and 16 guests, creating an intimate, community-oriented experience.

The typical liveaboard day follows a rhythm that divers quickly come to love. You wake early, often to the smell of fresh coffee and breakfast being prepared. The first dive is usually just after sunrise, when the marine life is most active. Back on board, you enjoy a hearty breakfast while the vessel moves to the next site. A second dive follows, then lunch, then perhaps a third dive in the afternoon. Some itineraries include night dives, offering a completely different perspective on the reef. Evenings are for sharing stories, reviewing the day's photos, and falling asleep to the gentle rocking of the ocean.

For serious divers, liveaboards are the ultimate way to experience PNG. They allow you to reach remote sites that day boats cannot access, to dive multiple times each day, and to immerse yourself completely in the underwater world without the distractions of daily life.

Best Liveaboard Vessels in Papua New Guinea

Several world-class liveaboard vessels operate in PNG waters. Each offers a slightly different experience, and the best choice depends on your budget, diving style, and preferred destinations.

MV Febrina: The Febrina is one of the most well-established liveaboards in PNG, operating for over 20 years. This 35-meter vessel accommodates 12 guests in comfortable cabins and specializes in itineraries that explore Kimbe Bay, the Witu Islands, and the remote reefs of the Bismarck Sea. The crew is exceptional, with decades of local knowledge, and the diving is world-class.

MV Spirit of Papua: A 30-meter sailing vessel that offers a unique experience combining diving with traditional sailing. The Spirit of Papua accommodates 10 guests and focuses on Milne Bay and the Trobriand Islands. The sailing aspect adds an extra dimension to the adventure, and the itinerary often includes cultural visits to remote villages.

MV Chertan: A more intimate option, the Chertan accommodates just 8 guests. This 22-meter vessel specializes in the Coral Sea region, including the reefs off the coast of Milne Bay. The small group size allows for highly personalized service and the flexibility to follow the best conditions.

MV Oceania: A newer addition to the PNG liveaboard fleet, Oceania offers modern amenities and spacious accommodations for 12 guests. The vessel covers all major PNG diving regions and is known for its excellent food and professional dive operations.

Walindi Plantation Resort Day Boats: While not a liveaboard, Walindi Plantation Resort in Kimbe Bay offers a land-based alternative with day boats that reach many of the same sites. For divers who prefer to sleep on land, this is an excellent option that still provides access to world-class diving.

Premier Diving Regions for PNG Liveaboards

PNG's waters are vast, and different regions offer different diving experiences. Most liveaboard itineraries focus on one or two regions during a week-long trip.

Kimbe Bay, West New Britain: Kimbe Bay is often described as the epicenter of marine biodiversity in PNG. The bay's geography—a series of volcanic calderas and fringing reefs—creates a sheltered environment that supports an incredible variety of marine life. Highlights include the pristine reefs of the Witu Islands, the wall diving at Father's Reef, and the wreck of the Japanese freighter "B-17" in Kimbe Bay. This region is ideal for divers of all levels, with sites ranging from shallow muck dives to exhilarating drift dives along walls.

Milne Bay: Milne Bay, at the southeastern tip of PNG, is famous for its muck diving. The sandy bottoms of the bay are home to an extraordinary collection of rare and unusual creatures—pygmy seahorses, flamboyant cuttlefish, blue-ringed octopus, ghost pipefish, and countless species of nudibranchs. But Milne Bay is also home to spectacular coral reefs, including the famous Deacon's Reef and the coral gardens of the Trobriand Islands. This region is a must for macro photographers and anyone who loves the strange and beautiful.

Witu Islands: Located north of Kimbe Bay, the Witu Islands are a remote volcanic archipelago that offers some of the most pristine diving in PNG. The islands rise steeply from the ocean floor, creating dramatic walls covered in soft corals. Pelagic life is abundant—schools of barracuda, jacks, and sometimes hammerhead sharks patrol the currents. The Witu Islands are accessible only by liveaboard and offer a true wilderness diving experience.

Trobriand Islands: The Trobriands, north of Milne Bay, are famous for their culture as much as their diving. This is where anthropologist Bronisław Malinowski conducted his groundbreaking research in the early 20th century. The diving here is exceptional, with pristine reefs, WWII wrecks, and a marine life that rivals any in PNG. Cultural visits to Trobriand villages add a unique dimension to liveaboard itineraries in this region.

Marine Life: What You Can Expect to See

The marine life of PNG is the stuff of legend. On any given dive, you might encounter:

  • Pygmy Seahorses: PNG is one of the best places in the world to find these tiny creatures, which live on gorgonian sea fans. There are several species, including the common pygmy seahorse and the rare Denise's pygmy seahorse.
  • Manta Rays: The reefs of Kimbe Bay and the Witu Islands are regular cleaning stations for manta rays. Encounters with these gentle giants are common during the dry season.
  • Sharks: While not as abundant as some Pacific destinations, PNG offers encounters with reef sharks, and occasionally hammerhead sharks, particularly in the Witu Islands and the Coral Sea.
  • Schooling Fish: The pelagic life in PNG is spectacular. Schools of barracuda, jacks, and fusiliers form living tornadoes that spiral around dive sites. Bumphead parrotfish, often in schools of 50 or more, are a regular sight.
  • Macro Life: For those who love the small stuff, PNG is a treasure trove. Nudibranchs of every color and shape, flamboyant cuttlefish, blue-ringed octopus, ghost pipefish, and countless other critters reward careful observation.
  • WWII Wrecks: PNG's waters are littered with wrecks from World War II—Japanese ships and aircraft that were sunk during the Pacific campaign. These wrecks have become artificial reefs, covered in soft corals and inhabited by everything from pygmy seahorses to massive groupers.

Best Time for PNG Liveaboard Diving

The best time for liveaboard diving in Papua New Guinea is during the dry season, which runs from May to October. During these months, the weather is generally stable, the seas are calm, and visibility is excellent, often exceeding 30 meters. The dry season also coincides with the best conditions for manta ray encounters and pelagic life.

May to October (Dry Season): This is the peak diving season in PNG. Expect calm seas, excellent visibility, and comfortable air temperatures. Water temperatures range from 26°C to 29°C (79°F to 84°F). This is the best time for liveaboards, though bookings should be made well in advance.

November to April (Wet Season): Diving is still possible during the wet season, but conditions are less predictable. Rainfall is higher, visibility can drop, and seas can be rough. However, this is also the time when some of the best muck diving conditions occur, as nutrients stirred up by the rain attract rare macro life. Prices may be lower during this period.

Water Temperature: Year-round water temperatures in PNG are comfortable, ranging from 26°C to 30°C (79°F to 86°F). A 3mm to 5mm wetsuit is usually sufficient, though some divers prefer a 7mm suit or a drysuit during the cooler months.

Cost of PNG Liveaboard Trips

Liveaboard diving in Papua New Guinea is a premium experience, and the costs reflect the remote location and high level of service. Expect to pay between $3,000 and $7,000 USD for a 7- to 10-night trip, depending on the vessel, cabin category, and itinerary.

What's Included:

  • Accommodation in shared or private cabins
  • All meals, snacks, and non-alcoholic beverages
  • 3-4 dives per day (including night dives)
  • Tanks, weights, and air fills (nitrox often available for an additional fee)
  • Guided dives with experienced dive guides
  • Port fees and marine park fees
  • Airport transfers on the day of embarkation/disembarkation

    • What's Not Included:

  • International flights to Papua New Guinea
  • Domestic flights to the embarkation point (usually Port Moresby, Hoskins, or Alotau)
  • Dive equipment rental (if not bringing your own)
  • Nitrox fills (typically $150-200 for the week)
  • Alcoholic beverages
  • Travel insurance (mandatory)
  • Gratuities for crew (typically $100-200 USD per week)

    • Booking Tips: Book your liveaboard at least 6 to 12 months in advance, especially for peak season. Many vessels offer early bird discounts for bookings made a year in advance. Consider traveling during shoulder months (May and October) for better availability and potentially lower prices.

    Typical Liveaboard Itineraries

    Liveaboard itineraries in PNG typically run for 7 to 10 nights. Here is a sample 8-night itinerary for a Kimbe Bay and Witu Islands trip:

    Day 1: Embarkation in Hoskins, West New Britain
    Board the vessel at Walindi Plantation Resort, complete dive briefings, equipment setup, and a check-out dive in Kimbe Bay.

    Day 2-3: Kimbe Bay
    Two days exploring the reefs of Kimbe Bay, including Susan's Reef, the B-17 wreck, and the spectacular wall dives at Father's Reef.

    Day 4-6: Witu Islands
    Overnight crossing to the Witu Islands. Three days diving the remote volcanic islands, with encounters with schooling pelagics, pristine coral gardens, and possible hammerhead shark sightings.

    Day 7: Garove Island
    Diving the caldera of Garove Island, a collapsed volcanic crater with dramatic walls and abundant marine life.

    Day 8: Return to Kimbe Bay
    Final dives in Kimbe Bay, including a night dive, before returning to Walindi.

    Day 9: Disembarkation
    Breakfast, packing, and transfer to Hoskins Airport for flights to Port Moresby.

    Diving Certification Requirements

    Most PNG liveaboards require divers to hold at least an Advanced Open Water Diver certification. The nature of liveaboard diving—multiple dives per day, potential for strong currents, and depths beyond 18 meters—makes this essential.

    Recommended Certifications:

  • Advanced Open Water Diver (or equivalent)
  • Enriched Air Diver (Nitrox) certification, which allows for longer bottom times
  • Deep Diver specialty, for those who want to explore deeper wrecks and walls
  • Underwater Photographer specialty, if you plan to capture the incredible marine life

    • Experience Requirements: Most operators recommend at least 50 logged dives before embarking on a PNG liveaboard. The diving can be challenging, and having solid buoyancy control, air consumption, and experience in different conditions will make your trip more enjoyable and safe.

    Medical Considerations: All divers must complete a medical questionnaire before diving. Any "yes" answers require a physician's clearance. Conditions such as heart problems, asthma, epilepsy, or diabetes may preclude diving or require special considerations.

    What to Pack for a PNG Liveaboard

    Packing for a liveaboard requires careful consideration. Space is limited, and you'll be living on a boat for a week or more. Here's what I recommend:

    Diving Essentials:

  • Mask, fins, snorkel (bring your own for comfort and fit)
  • BCD and regulator (rentals are available but bring your own if possible)
  • Dive computer
  • Exposure suit (3mm to 5mm wetsuit, or 7mm for cooler months)
  • Dive light for night dives and looking into crevices
  • Underwater camera system (PNG is a photographer's paradise)
  • Safety sausage and whistle
  • Save-a-dive kit (extra mask straps, O-rings, fin straps, etc.)

    • Clothing:

  • Lightweight, quick-dry clothing (you'll be living in casual wear)
  • Swimwear (several pairs)
  • Sun-protective rash guards
  • Warm layer for evenings (a fleece or light jacket)
  • Non-slip shoes or sandals for deck wear
  • Hat and sunglasses

    • Personal Items:

  • High-SPF, reef-safe sunscreen
  • Insect repellent (for evenings on deck)
  • Seasickness medication (even if you don't usually get seasick)
  • Personal toiletries (biodegradable preferred)
  • Reusable water bottle
  • Books or entertainment (though you'll likely be too tired to read)

    • Important Documents:

  • Passport with at least six months validity
  • PNG visa (obtainable on arrival for many nationalities)
  • Dive certification cards
  • Travel insurance details
  • Flight itineraries
  • Cash (PNG kina) for gratuities and incidentals

    • Expert Tips for First-Time Liveaboard Divers

    After guiding dozens of liveaboard trips, I've learned a few things that make the experience smoother and more enjoyable. Here are my top tips for first-time liveaboard divers:

    Manage Your Buoyancy: Good buoyancy control is essential on liveaboards, where you'll be diving multiple times a day in potentially challenging conditions. Practice before your trip. If you're rusty, consider a refresher course.

    Stay Hydrated: It's easy to become dehydrated when diving multiple times a day. Drink water constantly, even if you don't feel thirsty. Most boats provide water stations, so fill your bottle after every dive.

    Pace Yourself: The excitement of a liveaboard can lead to diving too much, too fast. Listen to your body. If you're tired, skip a dive. Fatigue leads to poor decision-making and increased risk of decompression sickness.

    Respect the Marine Environment: PNG's reefs are among the most pristine in the world. Help keep them that way by practicing good buoyancy, never touching coral or marine life, and using reef-safe sunscreen.

    Connect with Your Fellow Divers: Liveaboards create a unique community. Share meals, stories, and dive experiences with your fellow guests. Some of the friendships I've seen form on liveaboards have lasted for decades.

    Trust Your Guides: The dive guides on PNG liveaboards are among the most experienced in the world. They know the currents, the marine life, and the safety protocols. Listen to their briefings and follow their instructions.

    Bring Backup Gear: On a remote liveaboard, there's no dive shop around the corner. Bring spare mask straps, O-rings, fin straps, and batteries. A small "save-a-dive" kit can save your trip.

    Frequently Asked Questions: PNG Diving Liveaboards

    What is the best time to go on a PNG diving liveaboard?

    The best time for liveaboard diving in Papua New Guinea is from May to October during the dry season. Visibility is excellent, seas are calm, and marine life is abundant. Peak season is June through September.

    How much does a PNG liveaboard diving trip cost?

    PNG liveaboard diving trips typically cost between $3,000 and $7,000 USD for a 7-10 night trip, depending on the vessel, itinerary, and cabin category. This usually includes accommodation, meals, dives, and guides.

    What marine life can I see on a PNG liveaboard?

    PNG offers encounters with pygmy seahorses, manta rays, hammerhead sharks, schooling barracuda, pristine coral reefs, WWII wrecks, and exceptional macro life including countless nudibranchs and rare critters.

    Do I need prior diving experience for PNG liveaboards?

    Most PNG liveaboards require Advanced Open Water certification due to the nature of the dive sites, which can include strong currents and depths exceeding 18 meters. At least 50 logged dives are recommended.

    Which are the best diving regions in PNG for liveaboards?

    The premier liveaboard diving regions in PNG are Kimbe Bay in West New Britain, Milne Bay and the Trobriand Islands, and the Witu Islands off the coast of New Britain. Each offers unique diving experiences.

    How do I get to PNG for a liveaboard diving trip?

    International flights arrive at Jacksons International Airport in Port Moresby (POM). From there, domestic flights connect to Hoskins (for Kimbe Bay), Alotau (for Milne Bay), or other embarkation points depending on your liveaboard.

    What should I pack for a PNG liveaboard diving trip?

    Essential items include dive gear, lightweight clothing, reef-safe sunscreen, seasickness medication, and a good underwater camera. A detailed packing list is provided in this guide.

    A Final Reflection: Why Dive PNG?

    I have been diving PNG waters for over a decade. I have descended into Kimbe Bay more than a hundred times, and every dive still surprises me. A new nudibranch I've never seen. A pygmy seahorse I almost missed. A manta ray gliding out of the blue, close enough to touch.

    There is something about PNG diving that is different from anywhere else. It is the feeling of discovery—the sense that you are exploring waters that are still wild, still pristine, still full of mysteries. It is the warmth of the people, who welcome you into their villages and share their connection to the sea. It is the knowledge that you are diving in the heart of the Coral Triangle, the Amazon of the ocean, one of the last great wildernesses on earth.

    A liveaboard is the best way to experience this. It allows you to go deep, to immerse yourself completely, to wake up each morning to a new underwater world. It strips away the distractions of daily life and leaves you with nothing but the ocean, your dive buddies, and the incredible life beneath the surface.

    If you are a diver, PNG should be on your bucket list. And if you're not a diver yet, there is no better place to learn. The waters here are welcoming, the marine life is abundant, and the experience will stay with you forever.

    I hope to see you on a liveaboard one day. I hope to share a dive with you in Kimbe Bay, to point out a pygmy seahorse clinging to a sea fan, to watch you fall in love with this underwater paradise the way I did. Until then, safe diving, and may your bubbles rise to the surface in the most beautiful places on earth.
