Exploring the Active Volcano: A Visit to Mt. Tavurvur

March 15, 2026 By Xavier David 8 min read Volcano, Adventure, Rabaul

There are moments in life that leave you speechless. Standing at the base of Mt. Tavurvur, feeling the earth rumble beneath your feet, watching plumes of smoke rise against a brilliant blue sky—that was one of those moments for me.

As someone who grew up in East New Britain, I've seen Mt. Tavurvur countless times from a distance. But nothing prepares you for being up close. The sheer scale, the raw power, the undeniable presence of this active volcano—it commands your full attention and respect.

The Journey Begins

Our day started early in Kokopo. The morning air was cool, carrying that familiar scent of salt from the nearby ocean. My small group of travelers—a family from Australia, a couple from Germany, and a solo adventurer from Canada—gathered at our meeting point with excitement written on their faces.

"We're really going to an active volcano?" asked Sarah, the young mother from Sydney, her eyes wide with a mixture of excitement and nervousness.

I smiled. "We are. And I promise, it's an experience you'll never forget."

The drive from Kokopo to Rabaul takes about 45 minutes. As we wound our way through the lush countryside, I shared stories about the area's volcanic history. The road passes through villages where life continues as it has for generations—children walking to school, women selling fresh produce at roadside stalls, men heading out for a day of fishing.

But there's always Mt. Tavurvur in the background. Watching. Waiting. Reminding everyone who lives here that they share this land with something ancient and powerful.

A History Written in Ash and Fire

Mt. Tavurvur is part of the larger Rabaul caldera, a massive volcanic crater that collapsed after a major eruption thousands of years ago. The volcano has shaped not just the landscape, but the history and culture of this region.

The most devastating eruption in recent memory occurred on September 19, 1994. David, the founder of Bismark Touris, was a young man when it happened. He often shares stories of that day—the sky turning black, the ash falling like snow, the entire community coming together to survive. That experience shaped his deep respect for the land and his desire to share its story with visitors from around the world.

Today, walking through parts of old Rabaul, you can still see buildings buried up to their rooflines in volcanic ash. It's a sobering reminder of nature's power—but also of human resilience. The people of this region rebuilt their lives, adapted, and continue to call this beautiful, volatile place home.

The Ascent

We parked at the base of the volcano, where a local guide named Thomas was waiting for us. Thomas has lived in the shadow of Tavurvur his entire life. His father survived the 1994 eruption, and his grandfather before him had stories about earlier eruptions dating back to 1937.

"The mountain has a spirit," Thomas told us as we began our walk. "You must treat it with respect. Don't take anything from the mountain. Don't leave anything behind. Just be present."

The path to the volcano is not difficult, but it demands your attention. The ground beneath our feet was a mix of hardened lava, volcanic rock, and ash. In some places, steam rose from cracks in the earth—evidence of the heat still active beneath the surface.

We walked slowly, taking in the otherworldly landscape. The vegetation here is sparse, adapted to survive in harsh, mineral-rich soil. Hardy grasses, strange shrubs, and an occasional bird soaring overhead. It felt like walking on another planet.

After about 45 minutes of steady walking, we reached a viewpoint that offered an unobstructed view of the main crater. And that's when the mountain spoke.

The Mountain Speaks

A low rumble echoed across the valley. Deep. Resonant. The kind of sound you feel in your chest before you hear it with your ears.

"There it is," Thomas said calmly. "She's talking to us today."

A plume of white smoke rose from the crater, drifting lazily upward before catching the wind and dispersing over the sea. The group stood silent, phones and cameras forgotten for a moment, simply absorbing what we were witnessing.

Mt. Tavurvur is classified as a stratovolcano—a steep, conical volcano built up by layers of hardened lava, tephra, and volcanic ash. It's one of the most active volcanoes in Papua New Guinea, with its last significant eruption occurring in 2014. But it's never truly quiet. It breathes. It grumbles. It reminds you that beneath our feet, the earth is very much alive.

We spent nearly two hours at the viewpoint. The German couple, Klaus and Hannah, pulled out sketchbooks and began drawing the landscape. The Canadian, Mark, set up his camera on a tripod and waited patiently for the perfect shot. Sarah's children, Liam and Emma, asked question after question about volcanoes, their enthusiasm infectious.

I sat on a rock, took a deep breath, and felt grateful. Grateful to be here. Grateful to share this place with people who came from halfway across the world to see it. Grateful that the mountain chose to show us this gentler side of its power.

Beyond the Volcano: The Rabaul Volcanic Observatory

Before heading back, we made a stop at the Rabaul Volcanic Observatory. Perched on the slopes of another volcano—the older, dormant Mt. Vulcan—the observatory offers one of the best views of Tavurvur and the surrounding caldera.

Here, scientists monitor volcanic activity 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Their work is crucial for the safety of the 20,000 people living in the Rabaul area. David, who founded Bismark Touris, has worked closely with the observatory over the years, ensuring that our tours are always conducted safely and responsibly.

"The volcano is always active," explained Dr. Michael, one of the senior volcanologists. "But activity levels vary. Sometimes it's just steam and occasional tremors. Other times, we see increased seismic activity that tells us something bigger might be building."

He pointed to a seismograph, its needle scratching out a jagged line on a roll of paper. "See these spikes? Those are small earthquakes. We monitor them constantly. If we see a pattern that matches what happened before the 1994 eruption, we can give people 24 to 48 hours of warning."

The work they do is fascinating and vital. It's a reminder that while we can't control nature, we can learn to understand it—and that understanding can save lives.

Returning to Kokopo

As we drove back to Kokopo, the mood in the van was reflective. Everyone was processing what they'd experienced.

Sarah turned to me from the front seat. "I was nervous this morning," she admitted. "I didn't know what to expect. But this... this was incredible. The kids haven't stopped talking about it."

Mark, the Canadian traveler, spoke up from the back. "I've been to 47 countries, Xavier. I've seen some amazing things. But standing there, feeling the ground shake under my feet, watching smoke rise from an active volcano—that was different. That was real."

Klaus and Hannah nodded in agreement. "In Germany, everything is old and settled," Klaus said. "Here, you can feel that the earth is still being made. It's humbling."

I thought about their words as the sun began to set, painting the sky in shades of orange and pink. Mt. Tavurvur stood silhouetted against the evening light, still smoking, still watching.

What I've Learned

After years of bringing visitors to Mt. Tavurvur, I've learned a few things.

First, the volcano is different every day. Sometimes it's quiet, just a gentle wisp of steam. Other times, you can hear it rumbling from miles away. You can't predict what you'll see—you can only show up and accept whatever the mountain offers.

Second, this experience changes people. Standing before something so powerful, so beyond human control, puts things in perspective. Our problems seem smaller. Our connection to the natural world feels more real.

And finally, I've learned that Mt. Tavurvur isn't just a tourist attraction. It's a living part of our culture, our history, our identity. For the people of East New Britain, it's both a threat and a gift. It has destroyed homes and taken lives—but it has also shaped the land that sustains us, created the rich soil that grows our food, and given us a story that connects us to something ancient and eternal.

Planning Your Visit

If you're considering visiting Mt. Tavurvur, here are a few tips:

Best time to visit: The dry season (May to October) offers the clearest views. Early morning visits are best, before clouds build up over the crater.

What to bring: Sturdy closed-toe shoes, a hat, sunscreen, plenty of water, and a camera. A light jacket is useful in the morning when the mountain can be windy and cool.

Safety first: Always go with a licensed guide who knows the area and the current volcanic activity. The volcano is monitored constantly, and access may be restricted if activity levels increase.

Respect the mountain: Don't take rocks or ash as souvenirs. Don't leave trash behind. Treat this sacred place with the respect it deserves.

A Final Thought

As I write this, I can look out my window and see the silhouette of Mt. Tavurvur against the evening sky. Tomorrow, I'll take another group to see it. They'll feel the rumble, smell the sulfur, and leave changed in ways they didn't expect.

Not everyone gets to stand at the foot of an active volcano. Not everyone gets to feel the earth breathing beneath their feet. But if you ever have the chance, take it. It will remind you that this world is wild, beautiful, and wonderfully alive.

And it will leave you, as it left my group today, with a story you'll tell for the rest of your life.
