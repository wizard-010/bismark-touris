The Baining Fire Dance: Walking Through Embers Under Moonlight

March 24, 2026 By Emmanuel Marosi 25 min read Culture, Baining, Fire Dance, Tradition, East New Britain

The sun had already set behind the mountains when we began our journey. The road from Kokopo wound eastward, away from the coast, into the heart of the Baining Mountains. The air grew cooler as we climbed, the familiar scent of salt and tropical flowers replaced by the earthy smell of rainforest and woodsmoke.

I was taking a small group of travelers—a photographer from Tokyo, an anthropologist from London, and a young couple from Sydney who had seen videos of the Baining Fire Dance online and knew they had to witness it in person. None of us knew exactly what to expect. The Baining people are famously private, their ceremonies sacred, their traditions guarded. To be invited to witness a fire dance is a privilege few outsiders receive.

That night, under a moon that hung low and full over the mountains, I watched men walk through fire. Not over coals, not through carefully arranged embers, but through flames that rose to their waists, sparks flying like fireflies into the darkness. I watched them dance, their bodies painted with ochre and charcoal, their feet bare against burning ground, their faces hidden behind towering masks that seemed to come alive in the firelight.

I watched, and I understood something about courage, about faith, about the thin veil between this world and the next. I understood why the Baining Fire Dance has survived for centuries, why it endures, why it calls to people from across the world who have never heard the drumbeats echoing through the mountain valleys.

This is the story of that night. But more than that, it is the story of the Baining people, of their traditions, of a dance that is not really a performance at all, but a conversation with spirits that have walked these mountains since the beginning of time.

Who Are the Baining?

Before I tell you about the fire dance, I need to tell you about the people who dance it. The Baining are one of the oldest peoples of Papua New Guinea. They have lived in the mountains of East New Britain for thousands of years, long before the volcanoes erupted, long before the Europeans came, long before the town of Rabaul was built at the foot of their mountains.

The Baining are not a single tribe but a collection of communities scattered across the Baining Mountains and the surrounding lowlands. They speak different dialects, have different customs, live different lives. But they share something essential—a connection to the land that is deeper than memory, a culture that has survived centuries of change, and a ceremony that binds them together across generations.

Unlike the Tolai people who live along the coast of East New Britain, the Baining have traditionally lived in the mountains, isolated from the trade routes and colonial settlements that transformed the lowlands. They were farmers, growing taro and yams on steep hillsides, hunting wild pig in the dense forests, living lives that had changed little in centuries.

That isolation preserved their culture. While other peoples in Papua New Guinea were transformed by colonization, by Christianity, by the modern world, the Baining held onto their traditions. They adapted, as all peoples do, but they did not abandon the ceremonies that connected them to their ancestors.

The fire dance is the most famous of those ceremonies. But to the Baining, it is not a performance for tourists. It is not a spectacle designed to entertain. It is a sacred ritual, a communication with spirits, a test of courage and faith. It is something that must be witnessed to be understood, and even then, understanding comes slowly, if it comes at all.

The Journey to the Village

We had been planning this visit for months. The Baining do not welcome outsiders lightly. The fire dance is not performed on a schedule, not advertised, not offered for sale. It happens when the spirits call for it, when the village elders decide it is time, when the moon is right and the crops are harvested and the community is ready.

Our guide, a man named Peter who had lived among the Baining for years, had been watching and waiting. When word came that a dance would be held, he called us. "Tomorrow night," he said. "Be ready at sunset. Bring nothing but your eyes and your respect. And leave your expectations behind."

The drive from Kokopo took two hours. The road was rough, unpaved in places, winding through jungle that grew thicker as we climbed. We passed small villages, clusters of traditional houses with thatched roofs and walls of woven bamboo. Children ran to the roadside to wave. Women carrying heavy loads on their heads walked along the shoulder, their faces calm, their steps steady. Men sat in doorways, sharpening axes, weaving baskets, watching the world go by.

As we drove, Peter told us about the Baining. He spoke of their history, their customs, their relationship with the land. He spoke of the spirits that inhabit the mountains, the ancestors who watch over the living, the power that flows through the fire dance.

"This is not a show," he said, his voice serious. "Do not clap. Do not cheer. Do not take photos unless you ask and they say yes. You are here to witness something sacred. Act like it."

The photographer nodded, his camera heavy around his neck. The anthropologist took notes, her pen moving quickly across the page. The young couple from Sydney held hands, their excitement tempered by Peter's words. I sat in the front seat, watching the mountains grow darker as the sun sank behind them, wondering what we were about to see.

The Village at Dusk

We arrived as the last light faded from the sky. The village was nestled in a valley, surrounded by hills covered in jungle. A stream ran through it, its water black in the twilight, its sound a constant murmur beneath the evening sounds of insects and birds settling into their nests.

The village was alive with preparation. Women were cooking, the smell of roasting pork and taro filling the air. Children ran everywhere, their laughter echoing off the hills. Men gathered in groups, some painting each other's bodies with ochre and charcoal, others weaving leaves into elaborate headdresses, others simply sitting in silence, staring at the fire that had been built in the center of the village clearing.

The fire was enormous. It had been burning for hours, logs piled high, flames reaching toward the sky. The ground around it had been cleared, swept clean of leaves and debris. Beyond the fire, in the darkness, I could see shapes moving, figures emerging from the jungle, their bodies painted white, their heads crowned with towering masks.

Peter led us to a spot at the edge of the clearing. "Sit here," he said. "Do not move. Do not speak. Watch."

We sat. The ground was cool beneath us, the air heavy with smoke and anticipation. The villagers paid us no attention. They were focused on the fire, on the masks, on something we could not see or hear, something that was building in the darkness beyond the clearing.

The Masks

In Baining culture, masks are not costumes. They are living things. They are created from bark cloth and bamboo, painted with patterns that have been passed down for generations, infused with the spirits of ancestors, of animals, of forces that cannot be named.

The masks I saw that night were unlike anything I had ever seen. Some were tall, towering above the men who wore them, their faces elongated, their eyes hollow, their mouths open in silent screams. Others were round, like moons, their surfaces painted with spirals and zigzags, patterns that seemed to shift and move in the firelight. Some had bird beaks, others had tusks, others had faces that were not human, not animal, something else entirely.

Each mask had a name. Each mask had a story. Each mask carried the spirit of someone who had died, something that had lived, something that existed between worlds. The men who wore them were not themselves anymore. They had become the masks. They had become the spirits.

Peter whispered to us as the masks emerged from the darkness. "They have been in the forest for days," he said. "They have been fasting, praying, preparing. They have left their bodies behind. What you see now is not a man. It is a spirit. Treat it as such."

The masks circled the fire. They moved slowly at first, their steps deliberate, their bodies swaying to a rhythm that only they could hear. The drumming began—a low, steady beat that seemed to come from the earth itself, from the mountains, from the darkness beyond the firelight. The masks began to move faster.

The Dance Begins

It is hard to describe what happened next. Words are inadequate. Language fails. The dance was not a performance. It was not choreographed. It was not something that could be captured in photographs or described in sentences. It was something that had to be experienced, and even then, experience was not understanding.

The masks moved faster. The drums beat louder. The fire crackled and popped, sparks rising into the night sky, mixing with the stars. The dancers—if that is the right word—began to approach the fire. They moved toward it, not hesitating, not slowing, walking directly into the flames.

The photographer gasped. The young woman from Sydney grabbed her partner's arm. I held my breath, my heart pounding, my mind refusing to believe what my eyes were seeing.

They walked through the fire. Not over coals. Not through embers. Through flames. The fire was waist-high, hot enough to blister skin at a distance, and they walked through it, their feet bare, their bodies painted, their masks towering above the flames. They emerged on the other side, unharmed, untouched, still dancing.

I thought I was hallucinating. I thought the smoke had gotten to me, that my eyes were playing tricks, that I was seeing something that could not be real. But the others saw it too. We all saw it. Men walking through fire, again and again, their bodies unburned, their feet unblistered, their masks untouched by the flames.

Peter saw our faces. "You are wondering how," he said. "You are looking for an explanation. There is none. Or there are many. It does not matter. What matters is what you are witnessing. This is not a trick. This is faith."

The Meaning Behind the Fire

Later, after the dance was over, after the masks had returned to the forest, after the villagers had gone back to their houses, we sat with Peter and he told us what we had seen.

"The Baining believe that the world is full of spirits," he said. "Spirits of the forest, spirits of the river, spirits of the ancestors who came before. These spirits are everywhere, always, but usually we cannot see them. The fire dance is a way of calling them, of inviting them to show themselves, of communicating with them."

The masks, he explained, are the spirits. When a man puts on a mask, he becomes the spirit. He is no longer himself. He is something older, something greater, something that exists beyond the boundaries of ordinary life. The fire is a doorway, a passage between worlds. The dancers walk through it to show that they have passed from one realm to another, that they have touched the divine, that they have returned.

"The fire does not burn them because they are not fully in this world anymore," Peter said. "They are between. They are both here and there. The fire recognizes them as something other than flesh and bone. It does not consume them because they are not fully present to be consumed."

I asked him if he believed that. He looked at me for a long moment, his eyes reflecting the dying embers of the fire.

"I have seen men walk through fire a hundred times," he said. "I have never seen one burn. You tell me what to believe."

The Baining Way of Life

To understand the fire dance, you have to understand the Baining way of life. These are people who live close to the land, who rise with the sun and sleep when it sets, who grow their own food, who build their own houses, who live in communities that have existed for centuries. They are not wealthy by modern standards. They do not have electricity or running water or the conveniences that most of us take for granted. But they have something that many of us have lost.

They have connection. Connection to the land, connection to each other, connection to something greater than themselves. They know who they are. They know where they come from. They know what they believe. They are not searching for meaning because meaning is woven into the fabric of their lives, into every aspect of their existence, from the way they plant their gardens to the way they honor their dead.

The fire dance is an expression of that connection. It is not separate from daily life, not a performance that exists outside the ordinary rhythm of existence. It is part of that rhythm, a moment when the veil between worlds grows thin, when the spirits come close, when the community comes together to witness something that reminds them of who they are and what they believe.

For the Baining, the fire dance is not about entertainment. It is not about impressing visitors or preserving a tradition for its own sake. It is about maintaining the balance between worlds, about honoring the spirits, about ensuring that the community remains whole. It is a responsibility, a duty, a sacred trust passed down from generation to generation.

The Preparation

The dance we witnessed was months in the making. The decision to hold a fire dance is not made lightly. It comes from the village elders, the men and women who have spent their lives learning the traditions, who carry the knowledge of generations, who understand when the spirits need to be called and when they need to be left alone.

Once the decision is made, the preparation begins. The masks must be made. This is a sacred process, carried out by men who have inherited the knowledge from their fathers, who know which trees to cut, which fibers to weave, which paints to use. The masks are not art objects. They are vessels, containers for spirits that will inhabit them during the dance. They must be made correctly, or the spirits will not come, or worse, they will come but will not leave.

The dancers must prepare as well. They go into the forest to fast, to pray, to separate themselves from the ordinary world. They are not supposed to eat, not supposed to speak, not supposed to have any contact with the village. They are purifying themselves, emptying themselves, making themselves ready to be filled by the spirits.

The fire is prepared. Wood is gathered, stacked, arranged. The fire must be built in a specific way, using specific types of wood, arranged according to patterns that have been used for centuries. The fire is not just a source of light and heat. It is an altar, a doorway, a sacrifice.

The women cook. The fire dance is a time of feasting as well as ceremony. The community comes together, shares food, celebrates. The women work for days, preparing traditional dishes, roasting pork, boiling taro, making sago pancakes. The food is not just sustenance. It is part of the ceremony, an offering, a way of bringing the community together.

And then, when everything is ready, the village waits. They wait for the right moment, the right night, the right phase of the moon. They wait for the spirits to signal that they are ready. They wait for the ancestors to give their blessing. They wait, and when the time comes, they dance.

The Night of the Dance

The dance begins at dusk. The fire is lit, built up over hours until the flames are high enough to reach the sky. The villagers gather around it, sitting in silence, waiting. The children are quiet, their faces solemn, their eyes wide. They have been told what to expect. They have seen it before. But it never loses its power.

The drumming begins. It starts slowly, a single drum, a simple rhythm, the heartbeat of the earth. Other drums join in, each adding a layer of complexity, a new rhythm, a new voice. The drumming is not random. It follows patterns that have been passed down for generations, patterns that tell stories, patterns that call the spirits, patterns that guide the dancers.

The masks appear. They emerge from the forest, one by one, their bodies painted, their heads crowned with towering structures of bark cloth and bamboo. They move slowly, deliberately, their steps measured, their bodies swaying. They circle the fire, once, twice, three times, each time moving a little faster, each time coming a little closer.

The drums build to a crescendo. The dancers begin to move faster, their bodies contorting, their masks catching the firelight, casting strange shadows on the ground. They approach the fire, and for a moment, everything stops. The drums are silent. The villagers hold their breath. The dancers stand at the edge of the flames, waiting, waiting, waiting.

And then they move. They step into the fire, their feet bare, their bodies unprotected, and they do not burn. The flames lick at their legs, their chests, their masks, and they do not burn. They emerge on the other side, still dancing, still moving, still alive.

The drums begin again. The dancers move faster, crossing the fire again and again, each time emerging unscathed. The villagers watch in silence, their faces lit by the flames, their eyes following the dancers as they move through the fire, around the fire, between worlds.

I watched, and I thought about everything I had been taught. I thought about physics and chemistry and the limits of human endurance. I thought about what was possible and what was not. I thought about the rational explanations I had heard for fire walking—the protective layer of sweat, the quick steps, the properties of coals versus flames. But nothing I had learned explained what I was seeing. These men were not walking over coals. They were walking through flames. They were not stepping quickly. They were dancing, moving slowly, deliberately, their feet on the ground long enough to burn, their bodies exposed to heat that should have been unbearable. And they were not burning.

I gave up trying to understand. I let go of explanation. I let the experience wash over me, let the drumming fill my ears, let the flames fill my vision, let the dancers fill my mind. I stopped thinking. I just watched. And for a few hours, I understood something that I cannot put into words, something about the limits of knowledge, the power of faith, the thinness of the veil between what we call real and what we call spirit.

The Aftermath

The dance ended as the first light began to show in the east. The masks returned to the forest, their spirits leaving them as they walked back into the trees. The dancers collapsed on the ground, exhausted, their bodies covered in ash and sweat, their feet unburned, their skin untouched. The drums fell silent. The villagers began to drift back to their houses, their faces peaceful, their hearts full.

We sat in silence, not speaking, not moving, not sure what to say. The photographer had not taken a single picture. He sat with his camera in his lap, staring at the dying fire, his eyes distant, his mind elsewhere. The anthropologist had stopped taking notes hours ago. Her notebook was open, her pen was on the ground, her face was wet with tears. The young couple from Sydney held each other, their bodies trembling, their faces pressed together, their eyes closed.

Peter sat with us, waiting. When the sun rose over the mountains, painting the sky in shades of orange and pink, he stood up and stretched.

"You have seen something," he said. "You will carry it with you for the rest of your lives. Do not try to explain it. Do not try to prove it. Do not try to capture it in photographs or words. Just remember it. Let it change you. That is what the dance is for."

We drove back to Kokopo in silence. The road wound down the mountain, through the jungle, past villages waking to a new day. The sun was bright, the sky was clear, the world was ordinary again. But we were not ordinary. Something had happened to us, something we did not have words for, something we would carry with us forever.

The Future of the Fire Dance

When I think about the Baining Fire Dance, I think about what it means for the future. The Baining people have lived in the mountains of East New Britain for thousands of years. They have seen their world change in ways that would have been unimaginable to their ancestors. They have faced missionaries who told them their beliefs were wrong, governments who tried to change their way of life, outsiders who came to watch their ceremonies and left without understanding.

And yet, the fire dance survives. It survives because it is not a relic of the past. It is a living tradition, a practice that has meaning for the Baining people today. It is not preserved in museums or performed for tourists. It is practiced because it matters, because it connects the living to the ancestors, because it reminds the community who they are and what they believe.

There are challenges, of course. The young people are drawn to the cities, to the opportunities and temptations of the modern world. The old ways are being forgotten, the languages are being lost, the traditions are fading. But the fire dance remains. It is one of the last ceremonies that still brings the community together, that still passes knowledge from generation to generation, that still connects the Baining to the spirits of their ancestors.

I asked Peter, on the drive back to Kokopo, whether the fire dance would survive another generation. He was quiet for a long time, his eyes on the road, his hands steady on the steering wheel.

"It will survive," he said. "Not because we preserve it. Not because we perform it for visitors. Because we need it. We need the spirits. We need the ancestors. We need to walk through fire and know that something greater than ourselves is carrying us through. As long as we need that, the fire dance will survive."

I think he is right. The fire dance will survive because it is not a tradition that is being preserved. It is a practice that is still alive, still meaningful, still necessary. It will adapt, as all living things adapt, to the changing world. But it will not disappear. It cannot disappear. It is part of who the Baining are.

Can Visitors Witness the Fire Dance?

This is a question I am often asked. Visitors come to East New Britain hoping to see the Baining Fire Dance. Some are disappointed when they learn that it is not a regular performance, that it cannot be booked in advance, that it happens when the spirits call for it, not when tourists demand it.

But sometimes, visitors are invited. If you are lucky enough to be in East New Britain when a fire dance is held, and if you have earned the trust of the Baining people, you may be invited to witness it. It is not something that can be arranged. It is something that happens, or does not happen. And if you are invited, you must go with respect, with humility, with an open mind and an open heart.

If you do get to witness the Baining Fire Dance, here are some things to remember:

  • Do not take photos unless you ask and they say yes. The masks are sacred. The dancers are not performers. They are engaging in a religious ceremony. Treat it with the respect you would give any sacred ritual.
  • Do not clap or cheer. This is not a performance. It is not entertainment. Your approval is not required. Sit quietly, watch, let the experience wash over you.
  • Do not ask questions during the dance. The villagers are focused on the ceremony. They are not tour guides. Save your questions for after, when the dance is over, when the spirits have returned to the forest.
  • Do not expect to understand. You are witnessing something that has been practiced for centuries, a tradition that is woven into the lives of the Baining people. You are an outsider. You will not understand everything you see. That is okay. It is not for you to understand. It is for you to witness, to respect, to carry with you.

What I Learned

That night in the Baining Mountains changed me. I have been a guide for years. I have shown visitors the volcanoes of Rabaul, the beaches of Kokopo, the markets and museums and war memorials. I have told stories, shared history, tried to help people understand this place that I call home. But that night, I was the one who was changed.

I learned that there are things in this world that cannot be explained. I learned that faith is not about belief without evidence, but about experiencing something that transcends evidence. I learned that the Baining people are not primitive, not superstitious, not living in the past. They are people who have found a way to live with mystery, to honor the forces that shape their lives, to walk through fire without being burned.

I learned that the fire dance is not a spectacle. It is a prayer. It is a conversation with the ancestors. It is a reminder that the world is larger, stranger, more wonderful than our minds can grasp. It is a gift, given freely, offered to those who come with respect and leave with humility.

And I learned that sometimes, the best thing you can do is to stop trying to understand. To let go of explanation. To open yourself to experience. To sit in the darkness, watching the flames, listening to the drums, and let the mystery wash over you. That is what the Baining do. That is what they have done for thousands of years. That is what they will do for thousands more.

A Final Reflection

It has been years since that night in the Baining Mountains. I have been back many times. I have witnessed the fire dance again, have sat in the darkness, watched the flames, listened to the drums. Each time, it is different. Each time, it is the same. The masks are new. The dancers are new. But the spirits are the same. The mystery is the same. The fire does not burn.

I think about that night often. I think about the men who walked through flames, their bodies painted, their masks towering above them, their feet bare, their hearts open. I think about the villagers who sat in silence, their faces lit by the fire, their eyes following the dancers as they moved between worlds. I think about the photographer who never lifted his camera, the anthropologist who stopped taking notes, the young couple who held each other and cried.

I think about what Peter said, about carrying the experience with us, about letting it change us. I have carried it. It has changed me. I am less certain now than I was before. I am more open. I am more humble. I know that there are things in this world that cannot be explained, and I am okay with that. I am more than okay with that. I am grateful.

I am grateful to the Baining people for letting me witness something sacred. I am grateful to the spirits who came down from the mountains, who inhabited the masks, who danced through the fire. I am grateful for the mystery, for the uncertainty, for the questions that will never be answered.

And I am grateful for the fire, the fire that does not burn, the fire that opens doors between worlds, the fire that reminds us that there is more to this life than what we can see and touch and measure. The fire that calls us to dance, to step into the flames, to trust that something greater than ourselves will carry us through.

That is what the Baining Fire Dance teaches. That is what it has taught for thousands of years. That is what it will teach for thousands more. Step into the fire. Trust the spirits. Dance.
