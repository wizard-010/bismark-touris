Supporting Local Communities: Our School Visits

January 28, 2026 By Emmanuel Marosi 7 min read Community, Education, Giving Back, East New Britain

When David founded Bismark Touris in 2024, he had a vision that went beyond just showing visitors the beauty of East New Britain. He wanted to create a company that gave back to the community that shaped him. A company that turned tourism into a force for good.

Two years later, I'm proud to say that vision has become reality. One of the initiatives we're most proud of is our school visit program—a chance for our guests to connect with local children, donate much-needed supplies, and experience the warmth and joy of Papua New Guinea's youngest generation.

I remember the first time we organized a school visit. We had a group of Australian travelers who wanted to do something meaningful during their time in Kokopo. They asked if there was a way to connect with the local community. David immediately thought of the primary school near his childhood village.

What happened that day changed how we think about tourism. The children greeted us with songs and smiles. Our guests handed out books and pencils. The teachers showed us around their classrooms. And by the time we left, there were tears in everyone's eyes—not tears of sadness, but of connection. Of shared humanity. Of the simple joy that comes from giving.

Why Education Matters in East New Britain

Education is the key to opportunity. In East New Britain, as in many parts of Papua New Guinea, access to quality education can be a challenge. Schools often lack basic supplies—pencils, notebooks, textbooks, sports equipment. Classrooms can be crowded. Teachers work with limited resources, often buying supplies out of their own pockets.

But the children? They are eager to learn. Every time we visit a school, we see the same thing: bright eyes, wide smiles, hands raised high. The children want to be there. They want to learn. They dream of becoming teachers, nurses, pilots, engineers. They dream of a future that education can help them build.

That's why our school visits matter. It's not just about handing out supplies—though that's important. It's about showing these children that people care. That the world beyond their village is full of people who believe in them. That their dreams are valid, their potential limitless.

A Typical School Visit

Our school visits usually begin in the morning. We meet our guests at the hotel, load the van with boxes of supplies, and make the drive to the school. The journey takes us through villages and plantations, past cocoa trees and coconut palms, until we reach a cluster of buildings surrounded by green hills.

As we pull up, the children are already waiting. They line up along the road, waving and calling out greetings. "Good morning! Good morning!" Their voices are bright and eager. Some of our guests wave back, their faces breaking into smiles.

We are greeted by the head teacher, who leads us to a covered area where the students have gathered. They sit in neat rows, their uniforms pressed, their hair brushed. Some of them are nervous. Others can barely contain their excitement.

Then the welcome begins. The children sing for us—traditional songs, church hymns, even a few pop songs they've learned from the radio. Their voices rise together, pure and strong. I watch our guests as they listen. Some are smiling. Some are wiping away tears.

After the singing, the head teacher welcomes us formally. Then it's our turn. We introduce ourselves, explain who we are, and tell the children why we've come. Then we start handing out the supplies.

I've seen a lot of things in my life, but nothing compares to the look on a child's face when they receive a new notebook. They hold it like treasure. They trace their fingers over the cover. They open it carefully, imagining the stories they'll write, the sums they'll solve, the drawings they'll create.

Our guests hand out pencils, rulers, sharpeners, erasers. Some have brought sports equipment—soccer balls, netballs, jump ropes. Others have brought books, carefully chosen for the school's library. Every item is received with gratitude, with wonder, with joy.

More Than Just Supplies

But the supplies are only part of the story. The real magic happens in the moments between—the connections that form when strangers meet, when barriers fall away, when we recognize ourselves in each other.

I remember a young girl named Grace. She was maybe eight years old, with a shy smile and hair tied back with a red ribbon. One of our guests, a retired teacher from New Zealand named Margaret, sat down next to her. They looked at a picture book together, pointing at the images, sounding out the words. Grace's confidence grew with each page. By the end, she was reading aloud, her voice clear and proud.

Margaret was crying when she stood up. "I've been teaching for forty years," she said. "I've seen thousands of children learn to read. But this—this is special."

She and Grace exchanged addresses. They still write to each other. Grace sends drawings. Margaret sends letters. They are friends, connected across oceans, bound by a shared moment in a schoolyard in Kokopo.

The Impact on Our Guests

I've seen our school visits transform travelers. People who came to Papua New Guinea to see volcanoes and beaches leave with something far more valuable—a connection to the community, a sense of purpose, a renewed faith in the goodness of humanity.

One of our guests, a businessman from Singapore, told me that the school visit was the highlight of his entire trip. "I've been to 60 countries," he said. "I've stayed in five-star hotels and eaten at Michelin-starred restaurants. But standing in that schoolyard, watching those children sing, handing a pencil to a little boy who looked at me like I'd given him the world—that was the best moment of my life."

Another guest, a young couple from Canada, came back the next year. They'd been saving up, they told me, and they wanted to do more. They'd brought two suitcases filled with supplies—books, pencils, soccer balls, art materials. They spent a week with us, visiting three different schools, sharing stories, making friends.

"We wanted our children to understand what really matters," the mother said, gesturing to her two young boys, who were kicking a soccer ball with some local kids. "They'll remember this forever."

The Impact on the Schools

The schools, of course, are the real beneficiaries. The supplies we bring make a real difference. A box of pencils might seem small, but for a teacher who has been sharpening stubs for weeks, it's a gift beyond measure. A set of textbooks can transform a classroom. Sports equipment can change a school's culture, giving children an outlet for their energy, a reason to come to school, a chance to play.

But the impact goes beyond the physical. The visits themselves—the attention, the recognition, the validation—matter just as much. The children see that people from far away care about them. That their school matters. That their education is valued.

I've seen attendance increase after our visits. I've seen teachers more energized, more committed. I've seen the pride in the community, the sense that their children are seen, their school is recognized, their future is bright.

One head teacher told me: "When you come, you remind us why we do this work. You remind us that our children matter, that their education matters, that what we do here every day has meaning."

Schools We've Visited

Over the past two years, we've visited more than a dozen schools across East New Britain. Each one is unique, with its own character, its own challenges, its own bright spots of hope.

There's the small village school where the children walked two hours each way to get to class. We brought them shoes that year—enough for every child. The head teacher wept when she saw them. "They've never had shoes," she said. "They walk on rocks and mud, their feet cut and bruised. You've given them more than footwear. You've given them dignity."

There's the school in Kokopo where we helped build a library. Our guests donated books, and local builders constructed shelves. The librarian, a young woman named Martha, said it was her dream come true. "I wanted these children to have a place to read," she said. "A place where they can imagine other worlds, other lives, other possibilities. Now they have it."

There's the school where we sponsor a feeding program. Many children come to school hungry, their stomachs empty, their minds distracted. We work with the mothers in the community to prepare simple, nutritious meals. The difference is remarkable. Children who were tired and withdrawn come alive. They learn better. They play harder. They grow stronger.

The Ripple Effect

What I love most about our school visits is the ripple effect. One act of kindness leads to another. One moment of connection leads to lasting friendships. One small gift leads to a world of possibility.

I think about the children who have received our supplies. Some of them will become teachers, nurses, engineers, community leaders. They will remember that strangers came to their school, that people believed in them, that the world is full of kindness. And they will pass that kindness on.

I think about our guests, who return to their homes with new perspectives, new priorities, new friends. They tell their stories. They inspire others. They come back, year after year, bringing more supplies, more love, more hope.

And I think about our community, strengthened by these connections. The schools are better. The children are more confident. The parents are prouder. And we, the people of East New Britain, know that we are not alone. That the world sees us. That the world cares.

How You Can Help

If you're planning a visit to East New Britain, we invite you to join us for a school visit. It's a chance to see a different side of Papua New Guinea—the side that doesn't make it into the travel brochures, but the side that will stay with you long after you've gone home.

You don't need to bring much. A few notebooks. Some pencils. A soccer ball. A smile. The children will welcome you with open arms. The teachers will show you their classrooms. The community will open their hearts.

And you will leave changed. I promise you that.

If you can't make the trip, there are other ways to help. We accept donations of school supplies year-round. You can contact us to arrange a contribution. Every pencil, every book, every soccer ball makes a difference.

You can also spread the word. Tell your friends about our school visits. Share our stories. Help us build a network of support for these children, these teachers, these communities.

A Final Thought

As I write this, I'm looking at a photo from our most recent school visit. It shows a group of children, their faces bright with smiles, holding up the supplies we brought. In the background, a teacher is watching, her hand on a student's shoulder. In the foreground, one of our guests is kneeling, eye-level with a little girl, listening to her read.

This is what tourism should be. Not just sightseeing, but connecting. Not just consuming, but giving. Not just passing through, but staying—in memory, in spirit, in the lives we touch.

David started Bismark Touris with a dream of sharing his home with the world. But I think he'd say that the best part of that dream has been the chance to give back. To support the community that raised him. To build a future for the children who will carry East New Britain forward.

We're proud of our school visits. We're proud of the connections we've made, the lives we've touched, the hope we've shared. And we hope you'll join us. Whether you come to Kokopo or support us from afar, you can be part of this work. You can help us build a brighter future for the children of East New Britain.

Because every child deserves to learn. Every child deserves to dream. And every child deserves to know that someone, somewhere, believes in them.
