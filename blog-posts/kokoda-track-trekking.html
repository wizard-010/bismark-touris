The morning mist hangs low over the Owen Stanley Range as I stand at Owers Corner, the southern gateway to the Kokoda Track. Behind me is Port Moresby, the modern capital of Papua New Guinea. Before me is a 96-kilometer journey through some of the most rugged, challenging, and spiritually significant terrain on earth.

I have walked this track many times. Each time, I discover something new. Not just about the history that echoes through these valleys, but about myself. The Kokoda Track has a way of stripping away the unnecessary, of reducing life to its essentials: one step, then another. One ridge, then another. One village, then another.

In this guide, I will share everything I have learned about trekking the Kokoda Track. The history that makes this trail sacred to Australians and Papua New Guineans alike. The physical challenges that await. The practical details you need to plan your journey. And the intangible rewards that draw trekkers back, year after year, to walk this hallowed ground.

What is the Kokoda Track?

The Kokoda Track, also known as the Kokoda Trail, is a single-file footpath that winds 96 kilometers through the Owen Stanley Range in Papua New Guinea. It stretches from Owers Corner, about 50 kilometers northeast of Port Moresby, to the village of Kokoda in Oro Province. The track crosses some of the most rugged terrain on earth — steep mountain ridges, dense jungle, fast-flowing rivers, and deep ravines.

In 1942, this track became the stage for one of the most brutal and significant campaigns of World War II. Australian forces, supported by Papua New Guinean carriers known as the "Fuzzy Wuzzy Angels," fought a desperate rearguard action against advancing Japanese forces. For four months, soldiers on both sides endured unimaginable conditions — tropical diseases, torrential rain, mud that swallowed men whole, and the constant threat of ambush.

Today, the Kokoda Track is a pilgrimage route for Australians seeking to honor the courage of those who fought there. But it is also much more than that. It is a journey into the heart of Papua New Guinea — into villages that have changed little in centuries, into the lives of the people who still call these mountains home, into a landscape that demands respect and rewards determination.

The WWII Campaign: A Story of Courage

To walk the Kokoda Track without understanding its history is to miss the soul of the journey. The campaign that unfolded here between July and November 1942 was a turning point in the Pacific War. Japanese forces, having swept through Southeast Asia, landed at Buna and Gona on the northern coast of Papua. Their objective was to cross the Owen Stanley Range and capture Port Moresby, from which they could launch attacks on Australia.

Opposing them were the Australian 39th Battalion — young, poorly equipped, but fiercely determined. They were joined by Papua New Guinean carriers, men who carried supplies, evacuated the wounded, and fought alongside the Australians. The Japanese called them the "Fuzzy Wuzzy Angels" for their curly hair and their angelic compassion. Without them, the Australian forces would have been overwhelmed.

The fighting was savage. Men died from bullets, from bayonets, from disease, from exhaustion. The track itself was a weapon — steep, slippery, unforgiving. Men who fell were often left behind. The wounded were carried in stretchers, four carriers to a stretcher, through mud that reached their knees, up ridges that seemed to go on forever.

When the campaign ended, the Australians had held. The Japanese had been pushed back to the northern coast. But the cost was staggering. Over 600 Australians died. Thousands more were wounded or stricken with disease. The Japanese losses were even greater. And the Fuzzy Wuzzy Angels — their names are not recorded in the history books, but their sacrifice is remembered by everyone who walks this track.

Today, the Kokoda Track is dotted with memorials. At Isurava, a granite monument stands where the Australians made their most desperate stand. The words carved into the stone are simple: "Courage, Endurance, Mateship, Sacrifice." These are the values that defined the Kokoda campaign. And they are the values that every trekker carries with them as they walk.

Best Time to Trek the Kokoda Track

Timing your Kokoda Track trek is crucial. The weather in the Owen Stanley Range is unpredictable, but there are definite seasons that affect trekking conditions.

Dry Season (April to October): This is the best time to trek the Kokoda Track. The months of May through September offer the most favorable conditions with lower rainfall and more stable weather. Temperatures during the day range from 20°C to 30°C (68°F to 86°F), while nights can drop to 10°C to 15°C (50°F to 59°F). The rivers are lower, making crossings safer and easier. The trails are less muddy, though you should still expect mud — this is the jungle, after all.

Wet Season (November to March): Trekking during the wet season is possible but significantly more challenging. Rainfall is heavy and frequent, turning the track into a river of mud. Rivers rise quickly and can become impassable. Landslides are more common. The humidity is oppressive, and leeches are abundant. If you trek during this period, you must be prepared for delays, cancellations, and extreme conditions.

Peak Trekking Season (June to September): These months are the busiest on the track. The weather is at its best, and many trekkers, particularly Australians, use their annual leave to make the journey. You'll need to book well in advance — at least six months ahead for reputable operators.

Fitness Requirements & Preparation

Let me be direct: the Kokoda Track is not a walk in the park. It is one of the most challenging treks in the world. Do not underestimate it. I have seen experienced hikers struggle. I have seen elite athletes break down. I have seen people who thought they were fit discover that fitness on flat ground means nothing on the Kokoda Track.

What the Track Demands: You will be walking 6 to 8 hours a day, sometimes more. You will climb steep ridges — the "Golden Stairs" at the start is a brutal introduction, with over 1,000 steps carved into the mountainside. You will descend equally steep slopes, often on slippery mud. You will cross rivers, sometimes waist-deep. You will carry a daypack weighing 8 to 12 kilograms. Your legs will burn. Your lungs will work. Your feet will blister. Your mind will be tested.

Recommended Training: If you are serious about trekking the Kokoda Track, you should begin training at least six months in advance. Focus on:

  • Cardiovascular endurance: Running, cycling, swimming, stair climbing.
  • Leg strength: Squats, lunges, step-ups with a weighted pack.
  • Hill training: Find the steepest hills in your area and walk them repeatedly. Better yet, find a mountain and train on it.
  • Long walks: Build up to 6-8 hour walks with a weighted pack.
  • Mental preparation: The Kokoda Track is as much a mental challenge as a physical one. Prepare yourself for discomfort, for fatigue, for moments when you want to quit. Remember the words at Isurava: "Courage, Endurance, Mateship, Sacrifice."

Medical Considerations: You should have a thorough medical check-up before attempting the Kokoda Track. Conditions such as heart problems, severe asthma, or joint issues may make the trek unsafe. You will also need to consider:

  • Malaria prophylaxis — consult your doctor about the best options.
  • Vaccinations — typhoid, hepatitis A and B, tetanus, and others are recommended.
  • Travel insurance — ensure your policy covers helicopter evacuation from remote areas. This is essential.

How Much Does It Cost to Trek the Kokoda Track?

Trekking the Kokoda Track is not a budget adventure. The costs reflect the logistics of operating in one of the world's most remote environments.

Typical Costs: Most reputable trek operators charge between $2,000 and $5,000 AUD per person. This typically includes:

  • Permits and track fees
  • Experienced guides and porters (fuzo)
  • All meals on the track
  • Accommodation in villages (basic, often on the floor of village houses)
  • Airport transfers in Port Moresby
  • Emergency communication and evacuation coordination

What's Not Included:

  • Flights to and from Papua New Guinea
  • Flights from Port Moresby to Kokoda or Popondetta (approximately $300-500 AUD each way)
  • Accommodation in Port Moresby before and after the trek
  • Travel insurance (mandatory)
  • Personal gear and equipment
  • Gratuities for guides and porters (customary and appreciated)

    • Beware of Cheap Operators: If an operator offers a Kokoda trek for under $1,500 AUD, be very cautious. The low price often means corners are cut — inexperienced guides, inadequate safety equipment, poor food, or no contingency for emergencies. This is not a trek where you want to save money. Your safety depends on the quality of your operator.

    Typical Trekking Itinerary

    Most Kokoda Track treks are completed in 7 to 10 days. The traditional direction is from south to north — Owers Corner to Kokoda — following the path of the Australian retreat and subsequent advance. Here is a typical 8-day itinerary:

    Day 1: Owers Corner to Ioribaiwa (or Ua-Ule Creek)
    The trek begins with the Golden Stairs — a brutal ascent that sets the tone for the days ahead. You'll camp near Ioribaiwa Ridge, the furthest point of the Japanese advance.

    Day 2: Ioribaiwa to Nauro
    A day of steep descents and river crossings. You'll pass through the village of Nauro, where you can see remnants of the old war-era airstrip.

    Day 3: Nauro to Menari
    The track climbs and falls through dense jungle. Menari is one of the larger villages on the southern section, with a good campsite.

    Day 4: Menari to Efogi
    This is a challenging day with the climb to Maguli Ridge and the descent to Efogi. The village of Efogi is perched on a ridge with spectacular views.

    Day 5: Efogi to Templeton's Crossing
    You'll cross the famous Brigade Hill, site of one of the fiercest battles of the campaign. The memorial at the top is a place of reflection.

    Day 6: Templeton's Crossing to Isurava
    Templeton's Crossing to Isurava is one of the most beautiful sections of the track. Isurava is home to the main war memorial — a granite obelisk that honors the Australian and Papua New Guinean soldiers who fought here.

    Day 7: Isurava to Kokoda
    The final day's walk brings you to the village of Kokoda, where you can visit the Kokoda Memorial and the old airstrip.

    Day 8: Kokoda to Port Moresby
    A short flight from Kokoda airstrip returns you to Port Moresby, where you can rest, reflect, and celebrate your achievement.

    What to Pack for the Kokoda Track

    Packing for the Kokoda Track is an exercise in balance. You need enough to be safe and comfortable, but every extra gram will feel like a kilogram by day three.

    Essential Gear:

  • Sturdy, broken-in hiking boots (waterproof recommended)
  • Gaiters to keep mud and debris out of your boots
  • Lightweight, quick-dry hiking pants (2 pairs)
  • Moisture-wicking shirts (3-4)
  • Warm fleece or lightweight down jacket for evenings
  • Waterproof rain jacket and pants
  • Hiking socks (5-6 pairs, wool or synthetic)
  • Wide-brimmed hat and sunglasses
  • Headlamp with extra batteries
  • Water bottles or hydration bladder (minimum 2 liters capacity)
  • Water purification tablets or filter
  • Sleeping bag (rated to 5°C/40°F) and sleeping mat
  • Lightweight daypack (your porter will carry your main pack)

    • Health & Safety:

  • First aid kit with blister care (moleskin is essential)
  • Personal medications (carry in original packaging)
  • Malaria prophylaxis
  • Insect repellent with DEET
  • Sunscreen (high SPF)
  • Anti-diarrheal medication
  • Electrolyte replacement powders

    • Optional but Recommended:

  • Trekking poles — they save your knees on descents
  • Camera and spare batteries (charging is not available on the track)
  • Small notebook and pen for journaling
  • Personal snacks (energy bars, nuts, chocolate)
  • Lightweight sandals for camp

    • The Challenges: What to Expect

    Every trekker on the Kokoda Track faces the same challenges. Knowing them in advance does not make them easier, but it does help you prepare.

    Mud: The Kokoda Track is famous for its mud. In some sections, you'll be walking through mud that reaches your ankles, your knees, even your thighs. It's slippery, it's exhausting, and it gets into everything. Embrace it. You cannot avoid it.

    Leeches: These small, blood-sucking creatures are everywhere in the jungle. They drop from leaves, cling to your boots, and work their way into your socks. The best defense is gaiters and leech socks. Check yourself regularly. Salt or insect repellent can help remove them.

    River Crossings: You will cross dozens of rivers and streams. Some are shallow and easy. Others require wading waist-deep. During the wet season, rivers can become dangerous. Listen to your guides — they know the crossings and will advise when it's safe.

    Fatigue: The cumulative effect of day after day of hard walking is profound. You will be tired. Your legs will ache. Your feet will blister. You will question why you came. This is normal. This is part of the experience. Remember the soldiers who walked this track in combat boots, carrying rifles and packs, fighting a war. Your fatigue is nothing compared to theirs.

    Altitude & Humidity: The Kokoda Track is not high-altitude in the Himalayan sense — the highest point is about 2,200 meters (7,200 feet). But the combination of steep climbs, high humidity, and tropical heat takes a toll. Pace yourself. Drink water constantly. Listen to your body.

    The Local People & Fuzo

    One of the greatest privileges of trekking the Kokoda Track is the opportunity to meet the people who live along it. These villages — Kagi, Efogi, Menari, Nauro — have been here for centuries. The people grow sweet potatoes and taro, raise pigs, and live lives that are connected to the land in ways that most modern people have forgotten.

    The "Fuzzy Wuzzy Angels" of today — the guides and porters who accompany every trekking group — are the descendants of those who served in 1942. They carry your packs, cook your meals, show you the way. They are the heart of the Kokoda experience. Their knowledge of the track, their resilience, their humor, their kindness — these are what make the trek possible.

    When you walk the Kokoda Track, you are a guest in their homeland. Treat it with respect. Greet people in the villages. Ask permission before taking photographs. Learn a few words of Tok Pisin — the lingua franca of Papua New Guinea. A simple "moning" (morning) or "tenkyu" (thank you) goes a long way.

    At the end of your trek, it is customary to tip your guides and porters. This is not just a gesture of appreciation — it is a significant part of their livelihood. A typical tip is $50-100 AUD per guide and $30-50 AUD per porter, though you should follow your operator's recommendations.

    Frequently Asked Questions: Kokoda Track Trekking

    How long does it take to trek the Kokoda Track?

    Most trekkers complete the Kokoda Track in 7 to 10 days, covering approximately 96 kilometers from Kokoda to Owers Corner or vice versa. Some experienced trekkers complete it in 5-6 days, but this is not recommended for first-timers.

    How much does it cost to trek the Kokoda Track?

    Kokoda Track trekking costs range from $2,000 to $5,000 AUD depending on the operator, group size, and included services. This typically covers permits, guides, porters, food, and basic accommodation.

    When is the best time to trek the Kokoda Track?

    The best time to trek the Kokoda Track is during the dry season from April to October. The months of May to September offer the most favorable weather conditions with lower rainfall and safer river crossings.

    Do I need to be fit to trek the Kokoda Track?

    Yes, the Kokoda Track is a challenging trek requiring excellent physical fitness. You should be able to hike 6-8 hours daily over steep, rugged terrain. Cardiovascular endurance, leg strength, and mental resilience are essential. Start training at least 6 months in advance.

    What is the Kokoda Track?

    The Kokoda Track is a 96-kilometer trail through the Owen Stanley Range in Papua New Guinea. It was the site of a historic WWII campaign between Australian and Japanese forces in 1942 and is now a pilgrimage route for trekkers.

    What should I pack for the Kokoda Track?

    Essential items include sturdy hiking boots, quick-dry clothing, rain gear, sleeping bag, water purification, first aid kit, and insect repellent. A detailed packing list is provided in this guide.

    Do I need travel insurance for the Kokoda Track?

    Yes, travel insurance is mandatory and must cover helicopter evacuation from remote areas. Standard travel insurance often does not cover adventure activities like the Kokoda Track, so check your policy carefully.

    A Final Reflection: Why Walk the Kokoda Track?

    I have been asked this question many times. Why would anyone voluntarily walk 96 kilometers through mud and leeches, up ridges that seem to go on forever, across rivers that could sweep you away? The answer is different for everyone.

    Some walk to honor family members who fought there — grandfathers, great-uncles, men whose names are carved into the memorials along the track. Some walk to test themselves, to discover what they are capable of when comfort is stripped away. Some walk for the adventure, for the challenge, for the stories they will tell when they return home.

    But everyone who walks the Kokoda Track is changed by it. You will discover things about yourself that you did not know. You will forge bonds with your fellow trekkers that will last a lifetime. You will gain a new appreciation for what the soldiers endured — and for what the Fuzzy Wuzzy Angels gave.

    And when you stand at the end, at the Kokoda Memorial, looking back at the mountains you have crossed, you will understand. You will understand why people come back, year after year. You will understand why the Kokoda Track is more than just a trail. It is a place of memory, of courage, of transformation.

    I hope you will walk it. I hope you will find what you are looking for in those mountains. And I hope you will carry the spirit of the Kokoda Track with you, long after you have returned home.
