The first thing that struck me about Tufi was the silence. After the noise of Port Moresby—the horns, the shouts, the constant hum of a city pushing against its limits—the stillness of the fjords felt like a physical presence. We had flown in that morning, a small plane that descended through clouds to reveal one of Papua New Guinea's most spectacular landscapes: a coastline carved by ancient volcanic activity into a series of deep, narrow inlets that could easily be mistaken for the fjords of Norway. But this was not Norway. This was Oro Province, and the people waiting for me on the dock were not hotel staff in uniforms. They were my hosts, my family for the week ahead.

Mama Rose was the first to greet me. She was small, maybe sixty years old, with a smile that seemed to take up her whole face. She took my hand, held it in both of hers, and said something in her language that I did not understand. Then she laughed, pulled me toward her, and said in English: "You are tired. Come. Food is ready."

That night, I slept in a traditional house on stilts, the sound of the ocean below me, the stars brighter than I had ever seen them. I woke to the smell of woodsmoke and the sound of children's laughter. Over the next week, I learned to weave a bilum, the traditional string bag that every woman in the village knows how to make. I paddled a canoe through the fjords, stopping to snorkel in waters so clear I could see the coral fifty feet below. I sat with the men as they carved masks and told stories of their ancestors. I helped Mama Rose prepare sago, the staple food of the region, and learned that patience is the most important ingredient in anything worth making.

This is what a village homestay in Tufi offers. Not a resort experience. Not a sanitized version of culture. But something far more valuable: the chance to live, even for a short time, as the people of this place live. To be welcomed as family. To leave not as a tourist, but as a friend.

In this guide, I will share everything I have learned about village homestays in Tufi. The best villages, what to expect, how to get there, and most importantly, how to be a respectful and welcome guest in a community that has opened its doors to visitors from around the world.

What is Tufi?

Tufi is a small town located on the northeastern coast of Papua New Guinea, in Oro Province. It is the gateway to one of the country's most spectacular natural wonders: the Tufi Fjords. These fjords—known locally as "rias"—were formed by volcanic activity and rising sea levels that flooded ancient river valleys. The result is a coastline of deep, narrow inlets with sheer walls that rise hundreds of meters above the water, covered in dense rainforest that cascades down to the sea.

The Tufi area is home to several villages, each with its own character and traditions. The people of Tufi are known for their warm hospitality, their skill as fishermen and carvers, and their deep connection to the land and sea that have sustained them for thousands of years.

Unlike many parts of Papua New Guinea, Tufi has a well-established tourism infrastructure—but it is tourism on the community's own terms. The Tufi Resort, a dive-focused operation, has been operating for decades. More recently, village homestays have emerged as an alternative for travelers seeking deeper cultural immersion. These homestays are community-owned and operated, with the support of the Tufi Resort and local tourism organizations.

Why Choose a Village Homestay Over a Resort?

The Tufi Resort is excellent. I have stayed there many times, and I recommend it to divers and travelers who want comfortable accommodation with all the amenities. But a homestay offers something the resort cannot: immersion.

When you stay in a village, you are not a guest in a hotel. You are a guest in someone's home. You eat what they eat. You learn their rhythms—waking with the sun, working through the morning, resting in the heat of the afternoon. You sit with the women as they weave, with the men as they carve, with the children as they play. You learn that life here is hard, but also rich. That happiness does not come from things, but from people. From community. From belonging.

A homestay also puts your tourist dollars directly into the hands of local families. When you stay at the resort, some of your money goes to the local community, but much of it goes to the operators and suppliers. When you stay in a village, every kina you spend goes to your host family. It pays for school fees, for medicine, for the materials they need to continue their traditional crafts. It is a form of tourism that directly supports the people you have come to meet.

For me, the choice is simple. I have stayed in resorts around the world, and I have stayed in village homes. The resort experiences blur together. The homestays I remember forever.

Best Villages for Homestays in Tufi

Several villages in the Tufi area offer homestay experiences. Each has its own character and strengths.

Waghi Village: Located on the shores of one of the most beautiful fjords, Waghi is known for its hospitality and its spectacular setting. The village is easily accessible from the Tufi airstrip, making it a good choice for first-time visitors. Waghi families have been hosting visitors for years and have developed a system that balances the needs of guests with the rhythms of village life.

Karimba Village: Further into the fjords, Karimba offers a more remote experience. This is where you will find some of the most skilled carvers in the region. The men of Karimba create intricate masks, drums, and canoes using traditional methods passed down through generations. If you are interested in traditional art, this is the village to visit.

Kiwia Village: Perched on a ridge overlooking the sea, Kiwia offers spectacular views and excellent hiking opportunities. From the village, you can walk to waterfalls, through sago palm plantations, and along the ridgelines that separate the fjords. The women of Kiwia are known for their bilum weaving, and you can learn to make your own during your stay.

Mabiri Village: Closest to the Tufi Resort, Mabiri is a good base if you want to combine village life with resort amenities. The villagers have strong relationships with the resort and can arrange dive trips, boat charters, and other activities. Mabiri is also known for its excellent seafood—you will eat well here.

Whale Bay: A small settlement at the end of one of the longest fjords, Whale Bay offers the most remote and pristine experience. The journey there by boat is an adventure in itself, and once you arrive, you will feel a world away from everything. Whale Bay is ideal for travelers who want to truly disconnect.

What to Expect During Your Tufi Homestay

Understanding what to expect will help you prepare for your homestay experience and ensure that you are a respectful and welcome guest.

Accommodation: You will stay in a traditional village house, usually built on stilts with woven bamboo walls and a thatched roof. These houses are designed to be cool in the tropical heat. You will have a private room or a partitioned area within the family home. Bedding will be provided—usually a mattress on the floor with a mosquito net. There is no electricity in most villages, though some have solar lighting. You will be using a bucket shower and an outhouse toilet. This is basic living. Embrace it.

Food: You will eat what the family eats. Meals are built around staples like taro, sweet potato, sago, and rice, with fish or chicken when available. Food is cooked over an open fire or in a traditional mumu—an earth oven where food is layered with hot stones and banana leaves. The food is simple, fresh, and delicious. If you have dietary restrictions, let your hosts know in advance.

Language: The people of Tufi speak their own languages, as well as Tok Pisin, the lingua franca of Papua New Guinea. English is spoken by some, especially younger people and those who have worked in tourism. Learning a few words of Tok Pisin will be appreciated: "moning" (good morning), "tenkyu" (thank you), "gutpela" (good), and "nogat" (no).

Lifestyle: Village life follows the sun. People wake early, often before dawn. Morning is the time for work—gardening, fishing, cooking. Midday is hot, and people rest. Afternoon brings more work and, in the evening, time for sitting together, telling stories, and relaxing. You will be invited to join in the daily activities. Do not be shy. The more you participate, the richer your experience will be.

A Day in a Tufi Village

To give you a sense of what a homestay might look like, here is a typical day in Waghi Village, where I stayed with Mama Rose and her family:

5:30 AM — Wake Up: The roosters start crowing before the sun rises. I wake to the sound of Mama Rose moving around the kitchen, preparing the fire for breakfast. The air is cool and smells of woodsmoke and the sea.

6:00 AM — Breakfast: A simple meal of sago pancakes, fresh fruit, and tea. We eat sitting on the floor, the family gathered around. Mama Rose's grandson, about eight years old, is already dressed for school. He practices his English with me: "Good morning. How are you?"

7:00 AM — Morning Work: The men go to the gardens or the sea. Today, I go with Uncle John to check his fishing lines. We paddle a canoe through the fjord, the water glassy and still. The walls of the fjord rise above us, covered in green. We pull up the lines and find several fish. John smiles. "Good day," he says.

9:00 AM — Weaving with the Women: Back at the house, Mama Rose and her daughters are weaving bilums. I sit with them, my fingers clumsy, my thread tangling. They laugh, patiently show me again. Slowly, I begin to understand the rhythm of it. Over the week, I will make a simple bilum. It is not beautiful, but I am proud of it.

12:00 PM — Lunch: A meal of fish and taro, cooked over the fire. We eat together, and then the village rests. The heat is heavy. I lie on my mat and listen to the sounds of the village settling into silence.

2:30 PM — Afternoon Walk: We walk through the village, along the ridge above the fjord. The views are breathtaking. Mama Rose points out the places that matter—where her parents are buried, where the old men carve masks, where the sago is processed. She tells me stories of her childhood, of the war, of the changes she has seen.

5:00 PM — Evening Gathering: As the sun sets, the village comes alive again. Children play. Women prepare the evening meal. Men gather to talk. I sit with them, understanding only fragments of their conversation, but feeling the warmth of their company.

7:00 PM — Dinner: The evening meal is the main meal of the day. Tonight, we have chicken, cooked in a mumu with taro and greens. We eat by kerosene lamp, the light flickering, the children already sleepy.

8:30 PM — Sleep: The village is quiet. The stars are out. I lie under my mosquito net and listen to the sound of the ocean, a rhythm that has been here for millennia, that will be here long after I am gone.

Activities & Experiences in Tufi

Beyond the daily rhythms of village life, there are many activities you can enjoy during your homestay:

Canoeing the Fjords: The best way to explore the fjords is by canoe. Your hosts will take you out on the water, paddling through the narrow inlets, pointing out the places where the water is deep enough for diving, where the fish gather, where the birds nest in the cliffs. The silence of the fjords is profound—just the sound of the paddle and the calls of birds.

Snorkeling & Diving: The waters of the Tufi area are spectacular. Coral reefs thrive in the fjords, protected from the open ocean. You can snorkel directly from the shore and see a world of color and life. If you are a diver, the Tufi Resort can arrange trips to world-class dive sites, including the famous reefs of the Cape Vogel area.

Waterfall Hikes: Several villages have waterfalls within walking distance. The hike through the rainforest is an adventure in itself, and the reward is a cool, clear pool where you can swim. The waterfalls are sacred to the local people—respect their wishes about swimming and photography.

Traditional Craft Workshops: Many families offer workshops where you can learn traditional crafts. Weave a bilum with the women. Carve a mask with the men. Learn to make sago from the palm that has been the staple of this region for thousands of years. These are not tourist performances—they are real skills, passed down through generations.

Birdwatching: The forests around Tufi are home to an incredible variety of birds, including several species of Birds of Paradise. The best time for birdwatching is early morning. Your hosts can take you to good spots and help you identify what you are seeing.

Cultural Ceremonies: If you are lucky, your visit may coincide with a cultural ceremony—a wedding, a funeral, a celebration of the harvest. These are deeply meaningful events for the community. If you are invited to attend, consider it a great honor. Dress respectfully, follow the lead of your hosts, and do not take photographs without permission.

Visiting the Tufi Resort: While you are in the area, it is worth visiting the Tufi Resort. You can have a meal there, book a dive, or simply enjoy the views from the terrace. The resort also supports the village homestay program and can help you arrange activities and transport.

Cost of Village Homestays in Tufi

Village homestays in Tufi are remarkably affordable, especially compared to resort accommodation. Most homestays charge between $50 and $150 AUD per night, including all meals and basic activities. Some families offer discounts for longer stays or for groups.

What's Included:

  • Private room or partitioned area in a family home
  • All meals (breakfast, lunch, dinner)
  • Basic bedding and mosquito net
  • Cultural activities and guidance from your hosts
  • Boat transfers to and from the airstrip (often included)

    • What's Not Included:

  • Flights to Tufi
  • Dive trips or boat charters arranged through the resort
  • Purchases of crafts or souvenirs
  • Gratuities for your host family (customary and appreciated)

    • Payment: Most homestays are paid in cash, in PNG kina. Bring enough cash for your entire stay, plus extra for tips and purchases. There are no ATMs in Tufi. The closest bank is in Popondetta, which is several hours away by road.

    For a one-week homestay, budget $400-700 AUD for accommodation and meals. Add domestic flights ($600-1,000 AUD return from Port Moresby) and incidentals, and a week in Tufi will cost approximately $1,500-2,500 AUD total.

    How to Get to Tufi

    Tufi is accessible by air and, for the adventurous, by sea.

    By Air: The easiest way to reach Tufi is by domestic flight from Port Moresby. PNG Air operates flights to Tufi Airport several times per week. The flight takes about 90 minutes and offers spectacular views of the Owen Stanley Range and the coast. Book well in advance, especially during peak season.

    From the Airport: Tufi Airport is small—you walk off the plane onto the tarmac. Your host family will arrange to meet you at the airport. The drive to the villages is by four-wheel drive, often along unpaved roads. It can take anywhere from 20 minutes to an hour, depending on which village you are staying in.

    Alternative: Boat from Popondetta: For the adventurous, you can travel by boat from Popondetta to Tufi. This journey takes several hours and depends on weather and sea conditions. I recommend this only for experienced travelers with local guides.

    Best Time to Visit Tufi

    The best time to visit Tufi for a homestay is during the dry season, from May to October. During these months, the weather is more stable, with less rainfall and calmer seas. The skies are clearer, the fjords are at their most beautiful, and travel is easier.

    May to October (Dry Season): This is the peak season for visitors. Expect warm days and cooler nights. The sea is calm, ideal for canoeing and snorkeling. The forest is lush but trails are less muddy. This is the best time for photography and birdwatching.

    November to April (Wet Season): The wet season brings more rain, but also fewer visitors and lower prices. The rainforest is at its most vibrant, and the waterfalls are full. However, travel can be disrupted by weather, and some roads may become impassable. If you are flexible and prepared for rain, this can be a rewarding time to visit.

    Tides: The tides in the fjords are significant. Your hosts will know the best times for canoeing, swimming, and fishing based on the tide. Listen to their advice.

    What to Pack for Your Tufi Homestay

    Packing for a village homestay is different from packing for a resort. Here is what I recommend:

    Clothing:

  • Lightweight, quick-dry clothing (long pants and long sleeves for sun and mosquito protection)
  • Sarong or lavalava (worn by both men and women in the village)
  • Swimwear (modest—women should wear shorts or a t-shirt over a bikini when not swimming)
  • Warm layer for evenings (it can cool down after sunset)
  • Rain jacket (essential, even in dry season)
  • Sturdy sandals or reef shoes for water activities
  • Comfortable walking shoes or hiking boots for walks and hikes
  • Wide-brimmed hat and sunglasses

    • Personal Items:

  • High-SPF sunscreen (reef-safe recommended)
  • Insect repellent with DEET or picaridin
  • Personal toiletries (biodegradable preferred)
  • First aid kit (including blister care, antiseptic, pain relievers)
  • Water purification tablets or filter
  • Reusable water bottle
  • Headlamp or flashlight with extra batteries
  • Portable power bank (there is no electricity in most villages)

    • Optional:

  • Snorkeling gear (if you have it; rental is limited)
  • Underwater camera
  • Binoculars for birdwatching
  • Small gifts for your host family (school supplies, fishing hooks, fabric are appreciated)
  • Books or cards for quiet evenings

    • Cash: Bring enough PNG kina to cover your stay, tips, and any purchases. $500-1,000 AUD equivalent is a reasonable amount. There are no ATMs in Tufi, and cards are not accepted.

    Cultural Etiquette & Respect

    Being a respectful guest is the most important part of any homestay. Here are the key things to remember:

    Ask Permission for Photos: Never take photographs of people without asking first. Some people, especially elders, may not want their picture taken. A simple gesture—pointing to your camera and raising an eyebrow—is usually enough to ask. If they say no, put your camera away.

    Dress Modestly: In the village, modesty is important. Women should cover their shoulders and knees when walking through the village. A sarong over a t-shirt and shorts is appropriate. Swimwear is fine for swimming, but cover up when you are back on land.

    Respect the Sabbath: Many villages in Papua New Guinea observe Sunday as a day of rest. Do not expect to go fishing, hiking, or engaging in work on Sunday. Instead, attend church with your hosts—it is a meaningful experience and an honor to be invited.

    Participate: The best homestay guests are the ones who get involved. Help with the cooking. Try your hand at weaving. Go fishing with the men. The more you participate, the more welcome you will feel, and the more you will learn.

    Be Patient: Village life moves at a different pace. Things do not always happen on schedule. Meals may be late. Plans may change. Embrace the flexibility. This is not a five-star resort with a schedule of activities. It is real life, and real life is unpredictable.

    Gift Giving: It is customary to bring a small gift for your host family. Practical items are appreciated—school supplies for the children, fishing hooks, fabric, or rice and tinned fish. A contribution to the family's church or community fund is also a meaningful gesture. Do not give cash directly unless it is payment for your stay.

    Saying Thank You: At the end of your stay, thank your hosts sincerely. A small gift or a cash tip (in addition to your accommodation payment) is appreciated. More importantly, tell them how much their hospitality meant to you. These words matter.

    Frequently Asked Questions: Village Homestays in Tufi

    What are village homestays in Tufi?

    Village homestays are authentic cultural experiences where visitors stay with local families in traditional villages along the Tufi Fjords. Guests live alongside their hosts, share meals, participate in daily activities, and experience traditional Papua New Guinean life firsthand.

    How do I get to Tufi for a village homestay?

    Tufi is accessible by domestic flight from Port Moresby to Tufi Airport with PNG Air. Flights operate several times weekly. Your host family will arrange to meet you at the airport. The drive to the villages takes 20-60 minutes.

    What is the cost of a village homestay in Tufi?

    Village homestays typically cost between $50 and $150 AUD per night, including accommodation, all meals, and basic cultural activities. Longer stays and groups may receive discounts.

    What activities can I do during a Tufi homestay?

    Activities include exploring the Tufi Fjords by canoe, snorkeling in pristine waters, hiking to waterfalls, learning traditional crafts (bilum weaving, mask carving), participating in cooking, visiting sago palm plantations, and attending cultural ceremonies if available.

    Is Tufi safe for visitors?

    Yes, Tufi is a welcoming and safe destination. The communities are accustomed to visitors and are known for their hospitality. As with any travel, use local guides and follow the advice of your hosts. The area is remote and peaceful.

    What should I bring to a Tufi village homestay?

    Bring lightweight, quick-dry clothing, a sarong, sturdy sandals, hiking shoes, insect repellent, sunscreen, a headlamp, and cash (PNG kina). A detailed packing list is provided in this guide.

    Do I need to know the local language?

    English is spoken by some villagers, especially younger people and those who have worked in tourism. Learning a few words of Tok Pisin will be appreciated: "moning" (good morning), "tenkyu" (thank you), and "gutpela" (good).

    A Final Reflection: What the Village Gives You

    I have stayed in Mama Rose's house four times now. Each time, I return with something new. The first time, it was a bilum I had woven myself, clumsy and misshapen, but precious. The second time, it was a story—the story of her mother, who had survived the war, who had walked across the mountains with nothing but her children and her faith. The third time, it was a photograph: me, Mama Rose, her grandchildren, all of us laughing, the fjord behind us, the light perfect.

    This time, I am not sure what I will bring back. Perhaps nothing I can hold. Perhaps just the memory of sitting with her on the veranda, watching the sun set over the water, not talking, just being. Perhaps just the knowledge that somewhere in this world, there is a place where I am not a traveler passing through, but family.

    That is what a village homestay in Tufi gives you. Not souvenirs. Not experiences checked off a list. But connection. Belonging. The reminder that hospitality is not about what you have, but about who you are. And the people of Tufi, who have so little by the standards of the world, have this in abundance.

    I hope you will go. I hope you will stay with Mama Rose, or with another family in Waghi or Karimba or Kiwia. I hope you will paddle a canoe through the fjords, learn to weave, sit in the quiet of the evening and listen to the stories. I hope you will leave with more than you came with—not things, but something better. A piece of this place. A piece of these people. A piece of a world that is disappearing, but that still exists, here, in the fjords of Tufi.
