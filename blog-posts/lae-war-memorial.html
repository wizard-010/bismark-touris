Lae War Memorial: A Sacred Tribute to PNG's WWII Heroes

March 25, 2026 By James Waninara 6 min read History

In the bustling heart of Lae, Papua New Guinea's second-largest city, lies a place of quiet reflection and profound historical significance. The Lae War Memorial stands as a solemn tribute to the brave soldiers who fought and fell during World War II, a reminder of the sacrifices made on these shores more than eight decades ago.

As a local guide who has visited this sacred site countless times, I've come to appreciate not just its historical importance, but its role in connecting us to a pivotal chapter of our nation's story. Whether you're a history enthusiast, a descendant of veterans, or simply a traveler seeking to understand Papua New Guinea's past, the Lae War Memorial offers a moving experience that stays with you long after you leave.

A Strategic Location in the Pacific War

Lae's significance in World War II cannot be overstated. Before the war, Lae was a thriving administrative center under Australian administration, with a developing agricultural industry and strategic port facilities. When Japanese forces captured the town in March 1942, it became a major base for their advance toward Port Moresby and Australia.

The town's airfield, now Lae's airport, was one of the most heavily contested strips in the Pacific. The Battle of the Bismarck Sea, fought in March 1943 just north of Lae, saw Allied forces successfully intercept a Japanese convoy attempting to reinforce the garrison here. It was a turning point that marked the beginning of the end for Japanese operations in the region.

Australian and American forces recaptured Lae in September 1943 after fierce fighting, but the cost was high. Hundreds of soldiers lost their lives in the jungles and mountains surrounding the town, many buried where they fell, others laid to rest in what would become the Lae War Memorial.

The Memorial Today

The Lae War Memorial is located along Milford Haven Road, a short distance from the city center. Maintained by the Commonwealth War Graves Commission, the memorial grounds are immaculately kept—a garden of remembrance where peace has finally replaced the sounds of battle.

The memorial features a central stone monument bearing the names of soldiers who have no known grave, alongside rows of headstones marking the final resting places of Australian, Papua New Guinean, and Allied servicemen. The design is simple yet powerful, with manicured lawns and shade trees creating an atmosphere of tranquility.

One of the most poignant aspects of the memorial is the Memorial Wall, which lists over 300 soldiers who fell in the Salamaua and Lae campaigns but were never found. Walking along this wall, reading the names and the brief details of each soldier—their age, their home, their unit—brings the human cost of war into sharp focus.

Key Features of the Memorial

  • The Central Cross of Sacrifice - A distinctive feature of Commonwealth war cemeteries worldwide, this stone cross stands at the heart of the memorial grounds.
  • Stone of Remembrance - Inscribed with the words "Their Name Liveth For Evermore," this monument serves as a focal point for commemorative services.
  • Regimental Plaques - Bronze plaques around the perimeter honor the various Australian and Allied units that served in the region, from infantry battalions to air force squadrons.
  • Battle Honor Roll - A detailed account of the major engagements in the area, including the Salamaua-Lae campaign and the Battle of the Bismarck Sea.

Visiting the Lae War Memorial

The memorial is open to visitors daily from sunrise to sunset, free of charge. Here's what you need to know before your visit:

Getting There

The memorial is located on Milford Haven Road, approximately 2 kilometers from Lae's central business district. Taxis and PMVs (public motor vehicles) are readily available throughout the city, and most drivers know the location. If you're staying in Lae, it's a pleasant walk if you're comfortable with the tropical heat—just remember to bring water and sun protection.

Best Time to Visit

Lae has a tropical rainforest climate, meaning rain can occur at any time of year. The drier months from May to October offer the most pleasant conditions for visiting. If you're interested in commemorative services, ANZAC Day (April 25th) sees a dawn service at the memorial that draws hundreds of attendees, including veterans, their families, and dignitaries.

What to Bring

  • Comfortable walking shoes (the grounds are extensive)
  • Water bottle (hydration is essential in Lae's humidity)
  • Sun hat and sunscreen
  • Umbrella or rain jacket (especially during wet season)
  • Camera (photography is permitted, though please be respectful)

Respectful Visitation

The Lae War Memorial is a place of mourning and remembrance. When visiting, please observe these guidelines:

  • Speak quietly and move respectfully through the grounds
  • Do not walk or sit on grave markers
  • Remove hats when approaching the central memorial (a sign of respect)
  • If you're taking photographs, avoid capturing visitors who are paying respects
  • Consider leaving a poppy or small tribute if you're visiting on ANZAC Day or Remembrance Day

The Human Stories Behind the Stone

What makes the Lae War Memorial truly special are the stories it holds. Each name etched into that wall represents a life lived, a family waiting, a future cut short. Through my work as a guide, I've had the privilege of meeting descendants who travel from Australia, the United States, and beyond to honor their ancestors.

One such visitor was Margaret, an elderly Australian woman who came to see the grave of her uncle—a young soldier who had lied about his age to enlist. He was just 19 when he fell in the jungle outside Lae. Standing at his grave for the first time, she told me: "I've waited 70 years to say goodbye properly." It was a powerful reminder of why these memorials matter, not just as historical sites, but as places of personal healing and connection.

For Papua New Guineans, the memorial also honors our own. The Papua New Guinea Infantry Battalion, formed during the war, fought bravely alongside Australian forces. Their sacrifices are remembered here too, a testament to the deep bonds forged between our nations in those dark days.

Beyond the Memorial: WWII Sites in Lae

For those interested in exploring more of Lae's wartime history, several other sites are worth visiting:

  • Lae Airfield - The original wartime airstrip, now Lae's main airport, still bears traces of its military past.
  • Japanese Anti-Aircraft Sites - Remains of gun emplacements can be found on the hills surrounding the city.
  • Lae Botanic Gardens - Once the site of Japanese administrative buildings, now a peaceful green space.
  • Malahang Beach - Where American forces landed during the recapture of Lae.
