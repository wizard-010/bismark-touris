There is a special energy in Kokopo when a cruise ship is in town. The morning air buzzes with anticipation. Market vendors wake up a little earlier. Tour buses get their final polish. And at Simpson Harbour, a giant vessel slowly makes its way into the bay, carrying hundreds of visitors eager to discover this corner of the world.

For the guides at Bismark Touris, every cruise ship arrival brings the same excitement. It is more than just a job—it is an opportunity to share the beauty, culture, and warmth of East New Britain with people who have traveled across oceans to be here.

The Arrival: A Ship on the Horizon

The day usually begins before sunrise. The team arrives early, checking schedules and confirming with drivers and guides. By 6:00 AM, they are already at the wharf, watching as the ship slowly glides into Simpson Harbour.

From the shore, the ship emerges from the darkness—a floating city of lights, moving with quiet confidence across the calm waters. The first rays of sun catch its white hull, and suddenly the whole bay seems to come alive. Fishing boats begin their morning journeys. The market comes to life. And the town of Kokopo stirs, ready to welcome visitors.

As the ship docks, there is a moment of anticipation. Then the gangway lowers, and the first visitors step onto Papua New Guinean soil. Some are seasoned cruisers who have visited dozens of ports. Others are experiencing their first-ever cruise. Nearly all share the same expression—a mixture of excitement, curiosity, and wonder at the beauty of this place.

The Welcome: A Tradition of Hospitality

One of the most memorable moments is the traditional welcome ceremony. Local cultural groups gather near the wharf, dressed in their finest bilas—the traditional attire of feathers, shells, and tapa cloth. The sound of the kundu drums echoes across the harbor as dancers move in rhythm, telling stories that have been passed down through generations.

For many visitors, this is their first real taste of Papua New Guinean culture. Their eyes widen as they watch the dancers, some reaching for cameras to capture the moment. Children in the crowd bounce along to the drumbeats, while older visitors watch with quiet appreciation.

After the performance, visitors are welcomed with fresh flower garlands—the same garlands given to family and honored guests. It is a small gesture that carries deep meaning. It says, "You are welcome here. You are not just a visitor—you are part of the community, even if only for a day."

Exploring Kokopo Market: A Feast for the Senses

After the welcome ceremony, visitors fan out across Kokopo. Some join guided tours to explore the volcanic landscapes of Rabaul. Others head straight to the market, drawn by the vibrant colors and exotic smells.

The Kokopo Market is a sensory experience. Stalls overflow with fresh tropical fruits—papaya, pineapple, mango, watermelon. The sweet scent mingles with the earthy smell of freshly harvested vegetables. Vendors call out in Tok Pisin, their voices mixing with the laughter of children and the bargaining of shoppers.

Further into the market, visitors find the bilas stalls—handmade jewelry, carved masks, woven baskets. The craftsmanship is extraordinary. Each piece tells a story. The carvings depict legends. The woven patterns represent family histories. Behind every stall is an artist who learned their craft from their parents, who learned it from their parents before them.

Beyond the Town: Popular Shore Excursions

For those who want to venture further, a range of tours is available. Here are some of the most popular shore excursions for cruise ship visitors in Kokopo:

WWII Historical Tour: Explore the Japanese tunnels, visit the Bitapaka War Cemetery, and see the remains of the massive Japanese military base that once dominated Rabaul. This tour offers a profound glimpse into the Pacific War.

Volcano Adventure: Visit the Rabaul Volcanic Observatory and witness the smoking cone of Mt. Tavurvur. Learn about the 1994 eruption that buried much of Rabaul in ash and see how the town has rebuilt.

Cultural Village Experience: Visit a traditional village to see how local people live, learn about traditional crafts, and experience the warmth of Papua New Guinean hospitality firsthand.

Scenic Coastal Drive: Take a journey along the stunning coastline of East New Britain, with stops at breathtaking viewpoints overlooking Simpson Harbour and the surrounding islands.

The drive to Rabaul is particularly popular. The journey takes visitors through villages and plantations, past the old airport buried in ash from the 1994 eruption. At the lookout, visitors stand in awe, taking in the view of the bay, the volcanoes, and the town below. Some take photos. Some simply stand in silence, absorbing the moment.

Lunch: A Taste of Home

By midday, appetites are calling. Some visitors return to the ship for lunch, but many choose to stay onshore and experience local cuisine.

At the Kokopo Beach Bungalows, special lunches are arranged for guests. Tables are set under the palm trees, looking out at the turquoise water. The menu features local specialties—fresh fish grilled over coconut husks, taro cooked in coconut milk, fried bananas, and saksak (a traditional sago pudding).

Conversation flows easily as strangers become friends over a good meal. Some visitors share stories of their own homes—the cities they come from, the families they miss, the adventures that brought them here.

The Farewell: Until We Meet Again

All too soon, the day comes to an end. The ship's horn sounds, signaling that it is time to return. Visitors gather at the wharf, some laden with market purchases, all carrying new memories.

There are handshakes and hugs. Promises to return. Emails exchanged. Some visitors have tears in their eyes as they say goodbye.

The guides stand on the wharf, watching as visitors board the ship. They wave from the deck, calling out their thanks. Then the gangway rises. The ropes are cast off. Slowly, majestically, the ship begins to pull away from the dock.

Planning a Cruise Stop in Kokopo

For those planning a cruise that includes a stop in Kokopo, here is what to know:

Best Time to Visit: Cruise ships typically visit Kokopo from October to April, during the drier months in East New Britain. The weather is warm and generally pleasant for exploring.

Booking Shore Excursions: Booking shore excursions in advance is recommended to secure spots. Popular tours fill up quickly, especially during peak cruise season.

What to Bring: Comfortable walking shoes, sun protection, a hat, insect repellent, and a camera. For market visits, small denominations of Papua New Guinea Kina are useful for purchases.

Local Currency: The Papua New Guinea Kina (PGK) is the local currency. Some vendors may accept Australian dollars, but having PGK is recommended for market purchases.

Why It Matters

Every visitor who comes to Kokopo leaves with a piece of Papua New Guinea in their heart. They go home and tell their friends. They share photos, they share stories, they share their love for this place. Sometimes, they come back—bringing family, bringing friends, bringing the people they told about that magical day in Kokopo.

Tourism is about more than just showing people around. It is about building bridges. It is about showing the world the beauty, the warmth, the richness of the culture. Every time a cruise ship docks at Simpson Harbour, that opportunity is renewed.
